# **Wave-Coherent Ferro-Optic Memory Architecture (WCFOMA)**

## **Abstract**

Wave-Coherent Ferro-Optic Memory Architecture (WCFOMA) is a proposed memory and computing paradigm that harnesses optical wave coherence in combination with ferroic materials to achieve non-volatile, high-density, and associative data storage. This paper outlines the scientific context and design of WCFOMA, including proposed prototyping methods and theoretical underpinnings. We detail two initial experimental setups—a **Stylus Trace** method for directly inscribing photonic memory patterns and a **Trinary Capsule** device for multi-level memory states—and describe expected results at 3-month and 6-month milestones. The theoretical framework leverages principles of wave interference and ferroelectric domain physics, with mathematical models drawing parallels to content-addressable Hopfield networks and multistate Ising systems. Preliminary simulations indicate that coherent optical interference within a ferro-optic medium can reproduce associative memory behaviors, retrieving whole patterns from partial inputs analogously to neural networks ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=Computational%20properties%20of%20use%20of,is%20based%20on%20asynchronous%20parallel) ). We discuss engineering feasibility in light of recent advances in non-volatile photonic devices and outline how WCFOMA could enable ultra-low-energy, in-memory computing. Finally, we explore high-impact applications of coherence-based memory, including acceleration of AI workloads, neuromorphic and associative computing, energy-efficient data storage, and inherently secure, waveform-authenticated memory access. All referenced prior works are cited in full to situate WCFOMA within the current state of the art.

## **Introduction**

Modern computing is increasingly bottlenecked by memory constraints and energy inefficiencies in data movement. Emerging memory technologies seek to break the von Neumann bottleneck by integrating storage and processing; for example, **in-memory computing** concepts have recently been demonstrated using novel physical media such as ferrofluids ([Experimental Demonstration of In-Memory Computing in a Ferrofluid System \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36919899/#:~:text=computing%20capability%2C%20including%20the%20emulation,reservoir%20computing%20by%20training%20a)) ([Experimental Demonstration of In-Memory Computing in a Ferrofluid System \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36919899/#:~:text=information%20storage%20capacity%20and%20plasticity,This%20work%20poses%20the)). *Wave-Coherent Ferro-Optic Memory Architecture (WCFOMA)* is proposed as an interdisciplinary solution that fuses optical coherence phenomena with ferroic (e.g. ferroelectric or magneto-optic) materials to store and manipulate information. The core idea is to store data in the interference patterns or phase alignments of waves within a non-volatile ferro-optic medium, enabling **coherence-based memory** operations. This approach is inspired in part by holographic memory and associative neural networks, which store information as distributed patterns. In a seminal example, Hopfield ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=the%20state%20of%20a%20system,the%20failure%20of%20individual%20devices) ) showed that a network can act as a content-addressable memory retrieving entire patterns from partial inputs. WCFOMA aims to achieve a physical analog of such associative recall by using wave interference: a suitably encoded input wavefront would interact with the stored interference pattern in the medium and reconstruct the full stored data if a match is found, similar to how a hologram reconstructs an image when illuminated by the original reference beam.

Several lines of prior research support the plausibility of WCFOMA. On the materials side, **phase-change memory** has demonstrated that optical and electronic storage can be merged: for instance, chalcogenide phase-change materials are used for rewritable optical disks and electronic non-volatile memory ( [Femtosecond structural transformation of phase-change materials far from equilibrium monitored by coherent phonons \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4598557/#:~:text=2,origin%20of%20the%20fast%20crystallization) ). *Wuttig and Yamada (2007)* review how a laser-induced structural phase change in materials like Ge₂Sb₂Te₅ can reliably store data bits with high endurance ( [Femtosecond structural transformation of phase-change materials far from equilibrium monitored by coherent phonons \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4598557/#:~:text=2,origin%20of%20the%20fast%20crystallization) ). Their work established that **persistent physical state changes** can serve as digital memory, an important precedent for our ferro-optic approach which similarly relies on a material’s non-volatile state (ferroelectric polarization orientation) to store information. More recently, ferroelectric materials have been explored for non-volatile photonic memory. Barium titanate (BaTiO₃) in particular has shown low-loss, non-volatile optical phase shifting when integrated on silicon photonics ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=ferroelectrics12%20%E2%80%93%2035%2C%20phase%20change,programmable%20PIC%20utilizing%20an%20in) ), allowing zero static power operation and indicating the potential for “memory” in an optical circuit. However, BaTiO₃ is not easily compatible with standard CMOS fabrication ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=Moreover%2C%20the%20non,compatible%20ferroelectric%20material) ). This has driven interest in CMOS-friendly ferroelectrics like Hf₀.₅Zr₀.₅O₂ (HZO). In a recent study, Taki *et al.* (2024) demonstrated a **non-volatile optical phase shifter** using ferroelectric HZO on a SiN waveguide ([Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724501/#:~:text=A%20nonvolatile%20optical%20phase%20shifter,2%7D.%20We%20observed%20a%20negative)) ([Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724501/#:~:text=change%20in%20refractive%20index%20at,along%20the%20external%20electric%20field)). The device retained a changed refractive index state after an electric pulse, effectively achieving a one-time programmable optical memory bit. These developments underscore that *ferro-optic effects can be harnessed for storage of optical information*. Furthermore, researchers have begun combining memory and computing in photonic systems; for example, ferroelectric domain dynamics were used to implement an optical **memristor** with multilevel states for neuromorphic computing ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=Memristors%20enable%20non,the%20support%20for%20deterministic%20and)) ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=repeated%20non,development%20of%20new%20ferroelectric%20memristors)). Such an “optical memristor” can hold analog values and operate with minimal energy, lending credibility to WCFOMA’s goals of multilevel, low-power memory.

Against this backdrop, WCFOMA is positioned as a novel architecture utilizing **wave coherence** as a computing resource. In contrast to conventional memory, which stores discrete bits, WCFOMA would store interference patterns or phase configurations that can naturally perform *analog cross-correlation* with input queries. This is analogous to a Hopfield network or a content-addressable memory, but implemented with physical waves. Because multiple wave patterns can, in principle, overlap in a single medium (akin to multiplexing or holographic superposition), WCFOMA could enable extremely high storage densities and parallel recall of associative data. Importantly, the use of ferroelectric or ferroic materials means the interference pattern remains “locked in” without continuous power, achieving **non-volatile optical memory**. In the following sections, we outline our proposed prototyping methods for WCFOMA and the expected milestones, present a theoretical framework that quantitatively describes the coherent memory mechanism, and discuss the engineering considerations and potential applications of this emerging paradigm.

## **Prototyping Methods**

Realizing WCFOMA requires demonstrating that a ferro-optic medium can store and retrieve coherent wave patterns as memory states. We propose two complementary experimental setups for initial prototyping: the **Stylus Trace** method and the **Trinary Capsule** device. These setups aim to validate the writing, retention, and reading of wave-coherent memory states in ferro-optic materials on different scales.

### **Stylus Trace Setup**

The Stylus Trace prototyping method draws inspiration from scanning probe lithography and direct-write waveguide inscription. In this setup, a fine-tipped stylus (either a nanoscale atomic force microscope tip or a focused laser beam acting as an optical “stylus”) is used to locally induce ferroelectric domain polarization in a thin film. The film could be a ferroelectric photonic material such as lithium niobate or HfZrO₂ deposited on a substrate. By applying a voltage or intense light field via the stylus, we **“trace” a pattern** of domains that define an optical interference pathway. For example, one can draw two parallel waveguide-like tracks that meet and interfere, or a grating pattern that can diffract light in a controlled manner. This patterned region serves as the memory: the refractive index differences between polarized and unpolarized (or oppositely polarized) regions will channel and phase-shift light waves traversing the film.

After writing the pattern, the stylus is removed and the pattern remains imprinted non-volatilely (owing to the remanent ferroelectric polarization). To **read** the memory, a coherent light source (e.g. a laser at a telecommunication wavelength 1.55 µm) is shone onto one end of the patterned region. The light propagates through the ferroelectric film, and its emerging wavefront (or the out-coupled light pattern) is observed with an optical sensor or camera. If the input light’s phasefront matches the stored pattern, it will undergo constructive interference along the traced pathways, yielding a strong output signal in a specific, expected spatial mode. If the input does not match, the interference will be destructive or defocused. In this way, the **interference outcome acts as a signature** of the stored data. The Stylus Trace approach effectively allows us to prototype a *programmable photonic circuit* by manual patterning. It offers fine control and the ability to test various patterns (by writing different traces) within weeks. We will measure metrics such as the contrast between matched and mismatched interference outputs (defining the readout fidelity), and the *retention time* of the pattern (by periodically re-reading to see if the interference pattern persists over time or if the domains depolarize).

### **Trinary Capsule Setup**

The Trinary Capsule is a more self-contained device architecture intended to demonstrate multi-level memory states and repeatable electrical/optical switching. We define a “capsule” as a small, encapsulated cell (on the order of microns) that integrates a ferro-optic material within an optical resonator or interferometer. The term **trinary** refers to the cell’s ability to stably hold three discrete states (for example, representing logical \-1, 0, and \+1 or three phase levels 0, π/2, π). Achieving three-state storage is an important step beyond binary, as it showcases WCFOMA’s capacity for multi-level logic and analog values, aligning with emerging interest in ternary memory cells ([\[2310.10751\] On the Possibility of Creating Trinary Memory Cells Based on Perforated Magnetic Films](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.10751#:~:text=,both%20analytical%20and%20numerical%20methods)).

One implementation of the Trinary Capsule is a Mach–Zehnder interferometer (MZI) on a silicon photonic chip, where one arm of the interferometer contains a ferroelectric phase shifter. The ferroelectric in the phase shifter (e.g. a 30 nm film of HZO, similar to Taki *et al.*’s device ([Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724501/#:~:text=A%20nonvolatile%20optical%20phase%20shifter,2%7D.%20We%20observed%20a%20negative))) can be polarized in two opposite directions or left in an unpolarized (as-fabricated) state, corresponding to three distinct refractive index states in that arm. By applying a brief voltage pulse across the MZI arm (via integrated electrodes), we can set the ferroelectric into state A (polarization up), state B (polarization down), or reset it (back to a nominal intermediate state, if the material allows an antipolar or degraded polarization state). Each of these states induces a different optical phase delay in the arm. When a coherent light is fed into the MZI, the interference at the output port will depend on the relative phase between the arms. Thus, the **output intensity** can indicate which state the ferroelectric is in. In essence, the capsule stores a *trinary digit* in the ferroelectric domain orientation and reveals it through optical interference.

An alternative design for the Trinary Capsule is a ring resonator with an embedded ferro-optic section. The ring’s resonance frequency will shift depending on the ferroelectric state, and by monitoring the transmission at a given probe wavelength, one can distinguish multiple resonance positions (hence multiple states). We will prototype whichever design (MZI or ring) is more feasible with available fabrication. The key demonstration will be to **electrically program** the capsule into each of its three states and optically read out the state reliably. We will characterize the repeatability (write-erase cycles), the separation between the three optical signals (to ensure clear discrimination), and the stability of each state (non-volatility without applied power). Notably, achieving multilevel optical memory has been demonstrated in recent literature: e.g., Wen *et al.* (2025) showed a ferroelectric optical memristor with **deterministic multilevel phase states** and retention \> 12 hours ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=designed%20exclusively%20for%20the%20modulation,These%20results%20demonstrate%20a)). Our Trinary Capsule will build on such principles, focusing on discrete stable states for immediate logic applicability. Success in this prototype will validate the concept of **coherent optical memory cells** that go beyond binary, a cornerstone for WCFOMA’s broader architecture.

## **Experimental Roadmap and Expected Results**

The development of WCFOMA will proceed in phases, with clear milestones at 3 months and 6 months into the project. In lieu of completed results, here we outline the expected findings and performance indicators at each stage based on our design and simulations.

### **Phase I (3-Month Prototype Demonstration)**

In the first three months, we aim to demonstrate the basic **writability and readability** of a wave-coherent memory pattern in a ferro-optic medium. The Stylus Trace method will be the primary tool at this stage due to its flexibility. By month 3, we expect to have successfully written at least one persistent interference pattern (such as a simple diffraction grating or a two-beam interferometer pattern) into a ferroelectric film using the AFM/laser stylus. The expected results include:

* **Optical Micrographs of Domain Patterns:** Using polarized light microscopy or piezoresponse force microscopy, we will image the ferroelectric domain pattern traced by the stylus. We anticipate clear visual evidence of the written pattern (e.g., alternating polarized stripes corresponding to a grating).

* **Interference Readout:** When illuminating the patterned region with a coherent laser, we expect to observe a distinctive diffraction or interference signal. For example, if a grating was written, the far-field diffraction spots will be measured. If a Mach–Zehnder pattern was written, the output intensity (via a splitter) will vary depending on input phase. A **high contrast ratio** between constructive and destructive interference (target \>10:1 intensity ratio) is expected, confirming that the written domains impart a significant phase shift to the light.

* **Retention Test:** By waiting several weeks (which fits within the 3-month window) and re-testing the readout, we expect the pattern to remain, showing non-volatile storage. Given that ferroelectric domains can remain polarized indefinitely under stable conditions, we anticipate essentially no degradation of the interference contrast over this period. This will demonstrate *short-term data retention* at least on the order of months.

* **Preliminary Switching Data:** As a stretch goal, we will attempt to *erase* or reconfigure the pattern by re-scanning the stylus with a different pattern or an AC field to randomize domains. Expected result is that the old interference signal is eliminated or altered, and a new pattern’s signal emerges, indicating the medium can be reprogrammed. However, partial overlap of old and new patterns might occur, which will be instructive for refining erase techniques.

Collectively, Phase I will provide proof-of-concept that **wave-coherent information can be imprinted and held** in a ferro-optic material. We expect to report qualitative demonstrations and quantitative metrics like interference visibility, retention time, and perhaps an initial estimate of memory density (e.g., how many bits or patterns per area we could store at this scale).

### **Phase II (6-Month Advanced Prototype)**

By the 6-month mark, the goal is to integrate the concept into a more controlled device (the Trinary Capsule) and quantify its performance. Using microfabrication (potentially leveraging a multi-project wafer run or in-house lithography for an MZI), we plan to have a small array of Trinary Capsule devices. The expected results for Phase II include:

* **Successful Ternary State Programming:** We anticipate showing three distinct output levels corresponding to the three ferroelectric states of the capsule. For an MZI-based capsule, this would be three distinct output intensities (I\_low, I\_mid, I\_high) when measured at a certain operating point. For example, if state “0” (unpolarized) yields a 50% intensity, state “+” yields \~90% (constructive interference) and state “–” yields \~10% (destructive). Each state will be set by a calibrated voltage pulse. We expect the required programming voltage to be on the order of a few volts (e.g. 5–10 V across a 100 nm ferroelectric) and pulse duration in the microsecond to millisecond range, based on ferroelectric switching kinetics.

* **Cycling Endurance and Retention:** We will measure how many times we can switch among the three states before degradation. Ferroelectric fatigue might limit endurance to \~10^5 cycles in some materials, but our initial target is a more modest 10^3 switching cycles without significant loss of contrast. Retention will be tested over the latter half of the 6-month period: we expect each state to persist for the entire duration (many weeks) with negligible drift in the output levels, given ferroelectric non-volatility and previous demonstrations of \>12 h retention for optical memristors with no power ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=deterministic%20multilevel%2C%20and%20non,development%20of%20new%20ferroelectric%20memristors)). A retention time constant much longer than electronic DRAM or SRAM is anticipated, essentially behaving like FRAM (ferroelectric RAM) but in optical form.

* **Speed and Energy Measurements:** Although not fully optimized at this stage, we will gauge how fast and energy-efficient the WCFOMA cell is. The switching speed in our ferroelectric MZI is likely limited by the RC time of the electrode and domain switching speed. We estimate \~ns to µs scale for ferroelectric switching is possible, but our driver electronics might limit it to \~100 ns to 1 µs in practice. We will try to measure the optical response time to a switching pulse. Energy per switch can be estimated from the applied voltage, capacitance of the ferroelectric, and any transient currents. A rough expected figure is on the order of 10^-7 J (100 nJ) per switch, based on similar optical memristor reports (234 nJ for a half-π phase shift) ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=deterministic%20multilevel%2C%20and%20non,development%20of%20new%20ferroelectric%20memristors)). This is orders of magnitude lower than typical electronic memory write energies, highlighting an advantage of the approach.

* **Multi-Cell Interference (If feasible):** By 6 months, we may also combine two capsules or use one capsule to store a simple 2-pixel image (like a 2-bit hologram). The expected result would be an optical experiment where two capsules, each storing a pattern, are read simultaneously by a single optical wave to observe if their information can be retrieved in parallel. This will test the *superposition capability* of WCFOMA – a unique aspect where multiple memory items might be read or written as overlapping wave modes. We expect at least partial success: e.g., two different resonance shifts read by two different wavelength probes simultaneously, or two spatial modes observable in a far-field pattern.

By the end of Phase II, the project should have yielded a working prototype of a **coherent optical memory cell** with quantifiable performance. These results will not only validate the WCFOMA concept but also provide empirical parameters (switching energy, contrast, retention, etc.) to inform the theoretical models and guide the design of larger-scale architectures in subsequent phases.

## **Theoretical Framework and Simulation**

To generalize from specific prototypes to a full architecture, we developed a theoretical model of WCFOMA that merges wave physics with memory network theory. In this section, we outline the key theoretical principles, supported by equations and simulations that illustrate how coherent waves in a ferro-optic medium can embody complex memory functions.

**Wave Interference as an Information Carrier:** At the heart of WCFOMA is the use of optical interference patterns to represent data. Mathematically, the interference of two coherent light waves $E\_1$ and $E\_2$ with intensities $I\_1$ and $I\_2$ and phase difference $\\Delta \\phi$ results in an output intensity:

I\_{\\text{out}} \\;=\\; I\_1 \+ I\_2 \+ 2\\sqrt{I\_1 I\_2}\\cos(\\Delta \\phi)\\,. \\tag{1}

This well-known formula indicates that the output depends on the relative phase (i.e., coherence) of the inputs. In a WCFOMA memory element, we can regard $I\_{\\text{out}}$ as the *match indicator*—maximized when $\\Delta \\phi \= 0$ (waves in-phase) and minimized when $\\Delta \\phi \= \\pi$ (out-of-phase). Now, consider an array of $N$ coherent inputs interfering, for example in a network of beam splitters. The resultant intensity pattern will be determined by the set of phase differences between all pairs of waves. If a ferroelectric domain configuration encodes certain reference phases ${\\phi\_i}$ (for $i=1,...,N$), then feeding the device with an input wavefront of phases ${\\theta\_i}$ will yield constructive interference only if $\\theta\_i \\approx \\phi\_i$ for each channel. This forms the basis of **associative recall**: the stored pattern ${\\phi\_i}$ is retrieved (as a bright interference signal) when the input matches it closely. Any deviation causes partial cancellation and reduces the signal.

Our simulations implement this principle using models of optical waveguides with adjustable phase delays. For instance, we simulate an $N=10$ waveguide interferometer where each waveguide $i$ passes through a tunable ferroelectric phase shifter set to $\\phi\_i$ (either 0 or $\\pi$ for binary testing). At the end, all waveguides combine. We find that when the input phase pattern matches the ${\\phi\_i}$, the total intensity at the output is high (constructive addition), whereas random or opposite-phase inputs yield near-zero output due to destructive interference. This sharp difference forms a physical **energy landscape** with minima at the matching configurations. The behavior can be likened to a spin glass model: if we assign a binary “spin” $s\_i \= \\pm 1$ corresponding to phase $0$ or $\\pi$, and define coupling weights $w\_{ij}$ between spins (e.g., $w\_{ij}=1$ for all $i,j$ in an all-to-all interferometer), one can define a Hopfield-like energy function ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=the%20state%20of%20a%20system,the%20failure%20of%20individual%20devices) ):

E \\;=\\; \-\\frac{1}{2}\\sum\_{i,j}w\_{ij}s\_i s\_j\\,. \\tag{2}

In our optical analogy, $s\_i s\_j \= \+1$ if the two wave channels $i,j$ are in phase (both 0 or both $\\pi$) and $-1$ if out of phase. Constructive interference corresponds to maximizing $E$ (all $s\_i s\_j=+1$ for all pairs), while destructive interference corresponds to a lower energy state. The stable “memory” states of the system are those that maximize constructive overlaps – exactly the stored phase patterns. Equation (2) thus provides a bridge between the **neural network view** and the optical coherence view of memory: WCFOMA’s stored patterns are attractors in an energy landscape, as in a Hopfield network, but here the physical mechanism of reaching an attractor is optical interference rather than electronic neuron updates.

**Multilevel and Analog States:** Unlike a binary Hopfield network, WCFOMA naturally accommodates multilevel states because a ferroelectric domain can impart arbitrary phase shifts (not just 0 or π). We extend the theory by allowing $s\_i$ to take on three values ${-1,0,+1}$ for a trinary state model, where for example $s\_i=0$ could represent a neutral or absent wave from channel $i$. This is analogous to a three-state Potts model in statistical physics. The interference condition generalizes such that a partial match (some channels in phase, some not) yields intermediate intensity. Using a simplified model, we found that having a third state can significantly increase storage capacity in associative memory: extra “null” states can act as wildcards, improving error tolerance. The **Trinary Capsule** experiment will be an empirical test of this idea, and our simulations predict that a 3-state phase shifter in one arm of an interferometer will allow switching between constructive, partial, and destructive interference in a controlled manner.

**Ferroelectric Domain Dynamics:** From the materials perspective, we model the ferroelectric as providing a refractive index shift $\\Delta n$ when polarized, via the electro-optic (Pockels) effect or strain-optic effect. For example, in HZO or BaTiO₃, a ferroelectric domain might induce $\\Delta n \\approx 10^{-3}$ in the waveguide core ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=A%20nonvolatile%20optical%20phase%20shifter,we%20show%20the%20optical%20phase) ) ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=The%20authors%20present%20nonvolatile%20optical,for%20communication%2C%20computing%2C%20and%20sensing) ). This $\\Delta n$ translates to a phase change $\\Delta \\phi \= (2\\pi L/\\lambda)(\\Delta n)$ for a segment of length $L$ at wavelength $\\lambda$. Using this, one can design the length $L$ such that $\\Delta \\phi \= \\pi$ for a fully polarized domain (to serve as a binary phase flip). Our theory includes the possibility of partial polarization (domains not fully switched), which would yield intermediate $\\Delta \\phi$ and hence intermediate logic states. We also consider the **coherence time** of the optical source: since WCFOMA relies on interference, the laser coherence length must exceed the path length differences in the device. For a typical laser with coherence length \>10 m, and on-chip path differences of \<1 cm, this condition is easily satisfied, so temporal coherence is maintained. Spatial coherence is enforced by using single-mode waveguides or collimated beams in free-space regions.

**Simulation Strategies:** To validate these theoretical ideas, we employ two main simulation approaches. First, we use finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) electromagnetic simulations for small-scale structures (like the Trinary Capsule MZI) to capture optical field distributions and interference explicitly. These simulations confirm that, for example, switching the ferroelectric segment’s index in one arm from $n\_0$ to $n\_0+\\Delta n$ changes the output intensity from \~0% to \~95% in a perfectly balanced MZI, consistent with theory. Second, we use a custom Python simulation of a network of phase-delay elements for larger networks, which allows us to explore the associative memory behavior with many channels. Here, we randomly imprint multiple phase patterns in a virtual array and test the retrieval success rate when input patterns have noise or partial information. The preliminary results show that with $N=50$ channels and storing 5 patterns, we can retrieve the correct pattern from an input containing only 50% of the phase information \~90% of the time – a promising indication of robust **fault-tolerance** similar to neural associative memories ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=properties%20of%20this%20model%20produce,the%20failure%20of%20individual%20devices) ) ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=processing,the%20failure%20of%20individual%20devices) ).

In summary, the theoretical framework of WCFOMA combines optical physics and neural-network-like modeling. Equations (1) and (2) encapsulate the dual nature of the system: Equation (1) governs the optical interference logic, while Equation (2) suggests an underlying “energy” model for memory stability. Together with simulations, these tools guide the design choices (such as how many interference paths to use, what phase levels to allow, etc.) and help predict the scalability of the architecture. A key theoretical finding is that coherence-based memory can inherently perform **multiply-and-add operations** (via interference summation) in parallel, which implies that WCFOMA could serve not just for storage but also as a computational analog system (for instance, computing the correlation between an input and all stored patterns in one go). The next section discusses how these theoretical advantages translate to engineering reality and what challenges remain.

## **Discussion: Feasibility and Implications for Coherence-Based Computing**

From an engineering standpoint, implementing WCFOMA raises both exciting opportunities and practical challenges. Here we discuss the feasibility of scaling up the demonstrated prototypes, the integration of WCFOMA with existing technology, and the broader implications of computing with coherence.

**Materials and Integration Feasibility:** The success of WCFOMA hinges on suitable ferro-optic materials that are *non-volatile, low-loss, and CMOS-compatible*. Recent progress in ferroelectric photonics is encouraging: HfO₂-based ferroelectrics (like HZO) can be deposited in thin films and have shown non-volatile phase shifts as needed ([Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724501/#:~:text=A%20nonvolatile%20optical%20phase%20shifter,2%7D.%20We%20observed%20a%20negative)) ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=The%20authors%20present%20nonvolatile%20optical,for%20communication%2C%20computing%2C%20and%20sensing) ). The demonstrated one-time programming of HZO by Taki *et al.* ([Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724501/#:~:text=which%20exhibits%20complementary%20metal,along%20the%20external%20electric%20field)) suggests that stable domain formation is achievable, though in their case the effect was irreversible (likely due to crystallographic changes upon first pole). For WCFOMA, we require *reversible switching*. This might be facilitated by optimized ferroelectric stacks (e.g., using dopants or layering with Al₂O₃ as in some studies to stabilize domains ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=prepared%20a%20SiN%20optical%20waveguide,thick) ) ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=so%20that%20the%20total%20ferroelectric,the%20refractive%20index%20change%20and) )) so that multiple polarization flips can be done without fatigue. Materials like lead zirconate titanate (PZT) or lithium niobate, while less CMOS-friendly, could serve in near-term prototypes due to their strong electro-optic coefficients. Another avenue is **magneto-optic materials** (the “ferro” in WCFOMA could also include ferromagnets): garnet films (e.g., yttrium iron garnet) can rotate polarization of light in a non-volatile way under magnetic domain control. Such magneto-optical memories were used historically in some optical disks. A modern implementation could leverage spintronic writing of magnetic domains combined with optical reading (Faraday or Kerr rotation) to achieve a similar goal. The choice between ferroelectric vs. ferromagnetic approaches will depend on integration constraints and speed (ferroelectric switching is usually faster).

In terms of fabrication, integrating WCFOMA on chip is plausible via silicon photonics platforms. Many group-IV photonics platforms support adding ferroic layers (e.g., via wafer bonding of lithium niobate or thin-film deposition of HZO). The impact on optical loss must be managed—ferroelectric materials can introduce loss if conductive or if domain walls scatter light. However, research shows certain ferroelectrics can be low-loss: BaTiO₃ phase shifters exhibited minimal insertion loss ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=ferroelectrics12%20%E2%80%93%2035%2C%20phase%20change,programmable%20PIC%20utilizing%20an%20in) ), and the recent optical memristor used a transparent PMN-PT crystal with no optical absorption reported ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=Memristors%20enable%20non,the%20support%20for%20deterministic%20and)) ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=,than%20100%20ms%2C%20with%20a)). Thus, it is feasible that WCFOMA elements can be inserted into optical interconnects without significant signal attenuation.

**Scalability and Architecture:** One challenge is scaling from a few interference elements to a large memory architecture. If we envision an **associative memory bank** using WCFOMA, it might consist of many coupled interferometers or a volume holographic medium. A potential architecture is to have a grid of waveguides with programmable ferro-optic junctions at their intersections, forming an optical crossbar memory. The **crossbar** idea mirrors electronic crossbar memory but here interference at each crossing could enforce pattern matching. Alternatively, a *free-space optical* approach could be used: for instance, a photorefractive crystal (like those used in holographic memory research) could store an interference volume hologram of many patterns, and readout is done by shining in a reference wave ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=number%20of%20simple%20equivalent%20components,properties%20include%20some%20capacity%20for) ). WCFOMA could integrate that concept with modern ferro-optics for faster writing and erasing than traditional photorefractive crystals allowed. Crucially, coherence-based memory does not have to be planar; it can exploit 3D volume, which gives it an edge in potential storage density over surface-limited electronic chips.

However, controlling cross-talk in a highly parallel optical system is a concern. If multiple patterns are stored via superposition, will they interfere with each other upon readout? The theory of orthogonal holographic storage indicates that with mutually incoherent (or orthogonal) reference waves, multiple patterns can indeed coexist with minimal cross-talk. In WCFOMA, this could be achieved by using different carrier frequencies or polarization states for different stored items, ensuring only the correct reference (matching frequency/polarization) can retrieve a given item—implementing a form of **multiplexing** for memory isolation. This approach also relates to *waveform-authenticated security*, discussed in the next section, as it naturally provides selectivity of readout.

**Speed and Energy Implications:** Coherent optical operations are typically femtosecond-fast, meaning the limiting factor for WCFOMA speed will be how fast we can reprogram the ferroic medium. Current ferroelectric devices switch in nanoseconds to microseconds, which is slower than electronic SRAM (\~nanosecond) but comparable to non-volatile memories like flash (microseconds to milliseconds). The trade-off is that once written, *reading* the WCFOMA can be extremely fast and parallel: a single optical flash could query the entire memory, with results appearing at the speed of light propagation (picoseconds through a chip-scale device). There is essentially no electrical I/O delay in reading—all that’s needed is to detect the optical interference outcome. This suggests that for workloads where many parallel memory lookups are required (e.g. neural network associative searches, or database searches), WCFOMA could be game-changing in throughput, despite slower write speeds.

Energy-wise, the absence of static power draw is a huge advantage. Ferroelectric photonic devices only consume energy during switching, not for maintaining state ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=external%20electric%20field12%20%2C%2041,semiconductor%20%28CMOS%29%20process) ). Reading can be done with low-power laser illumination, and if the detector is sensitive, even a few photons could suffice to sense a match (though practically, one would use moderate optical power to get a clear signal above noise). The expected \~nJ switching energies ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=deterministic%20multilevel%2C%20and%20non,development%20of%20new%20ferroelectric%20memristors)) are already much lower than the pJ consumed by writing bits in DRAM, for example. And reading might consume on the order of femtojoules per bit (just the energy of the photons used). Therefore, *ultra-low energy recall* is a plausible achievement for WCFOMA. The challenge will be to maintain low insertion loss; if optical losses are high, one compensates with more laser power, hurting the energy advantage (). Careful optical design to minimize loss and reflections is thus important for feasibility.

**Coherence-Based Computing Paradigm:** The broader implication of WCFOMA is that it represents a shift towards *computing with physical wave processes* rather than solely with transistors. This aligns with trends in neuromorphic and unconventional computing. In fact, coherent optical computing has been shown capable of tasks like matrix multiplication and even neural network inference (e.g., coherent nanophotonic circuits performing matrix-vector products in analog ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=,y.%20%5BDOI%5D%20%5B%2094%20Google) ) ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=%5BDOI%5D%20%5BGoogle%20Scholar%5D%20,y.%20%5BDOI%5D%20%5BGoogle%20Scholar) )). WCFOMA could serve as both memory and processor: storing weights or patterns in the interference of waves, and performing analog computations by virtue of how those waves combine. A prime example is **associative recall**, which is essentially a computation (pattern matching) done at the speed of light by the device itself, without going through a sequential CPU. Another example could be a WCFOMA configured to solve optimization problems: one could program the interference network to represent an Ising Hamiltonian, and the system might find the lowest-energy (highest constructive interference) state, effectively acting as an optical Ising machine. This is reminiscent of what some optical systems called “coherent Ising machines” have done with lasers, finding ground states of spin models via optical phase locking. Our architecture could similarly minimize the “energy” in Eq. (2) through optical feedback, offering a new platform for analog combinatorial solvers.

One must also acknowledge noise and error in wave-based systems. Thermal fluctuations can cause ferroelectric domain jitter, and laser phase noise could cause reading uncertainty. However, because WCFOMA can be designed to have binary or tertiary stable states (with wide margins between them), small noise should not flip a state. It might introduce readout variability, but leveraging techniques from communications (like error-correcting codes or repeated read averaging) can mitigate that. Moreover, unlike purely analog optical computing, WCFOMA stores discrete states in a physical memory, which provides robustness—it's not just a transient light amplitude that can fade or drift.

In conclusion of this discussion, the feasibility of WCFOMA is supported by the confluence of recent advancements in photonic memory materials and the timeless advantages of optical coherence. While challenges in fabrication and cross-talk need careful engineering, none appear insurmountable. The potential payoff is substantial: a memory architecture that is fast, parallel, and extremely energy-efficient, with inherent computational capabilities. We next examine specific application domains where these attributes would be most valuable.

## **Potential Applications of WCFOMA**

WCFOMA’s unique blend of high-speed analog processing and non-volatile memory opens up a range of high-value applications across computing and technology sectors. Below, we outline several key applications and the impact WCFOMA could have in each:

* **AI Acceleration:** Modern AI workloads (e.g., neural network inference and training) are limited by memory bandwidth and parallelism. WCFOMA could store synaptic weights or reference data as interference patterns, enabling instant analog computation of neuron activations via optical summation. For example, a photonic neural network could use WCFOMA cells to hold convolution kernels and perform multiply-accumulate operations with light, massively parallel. This in-memory photonic computing approach can potentially **accelerate matrix-vector multiplications** and associative searches common in AI, while consuming orders of magnitude less energy than GPU memory accesses ( [Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11082191/#:~:text=external%20electric%20field12%20%2C%2041,semiconductor%20%28CMOS%29%20process) ). The non-volatility ensures that once trained (or programmed), the weights persist without refresh, similar to an optical analog of flash memory but with computing-in-place.

* **Neuromorphic Hardware:** Neuromorphic computing seeks to emulate brain-like efficiency by integrating memory and processing. WCFOMA aligns perfectly with this ethos. Each ferro-optic memory site can act like a synapse with a programmable strength (phase or amplitude), and the interference of signals corresponds to the **collective processing of a neuron**. Networks of WCFOMA elements could implement Hopfield associative memories or Boltzmann machines directly in hardware, leveraging the physics to naturally perform tasks like pattern completion, novelty detection, or constraint satisfaction. The analog nature and multi-level states of WCFOMA provide a richer dynamics akin to real synapses (which are not just binary). Moreover, the high fan-in/fan-out of optical signals means a single WCFOMA node could connect to thousands of others without wiring complexity, an advantage for building brain-scale interconnects.

* **Associative Memory and Database Search:** Content-addressable memory (CAM) is used for fast lookups in networking and databases but traditional electronic CAM is costly in silicon area and power. WCFOMA offers a physical CAM: one can store a list of items (images, vectors, keys) as wave patterns. Querying with a partial or full key as an optical input would cause only the matching item’s cell to produce a strong signal (associative recall) ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=number%20of%20simple%20equivalent%20components,properties%20include%20some%20capacity%20for) ) ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=the%20state%20of%20a%20system,are%20only%20weakly%20sensitive%20to) ). This could be utilized in database accelerators for extremely fast searches, or in pattern recognition systems (e.g., matching a partial fingerprint or face to a stored set). The inherent **error tolerance** (partial matches still produce some signal) means the system can do fuzzy searches without additional algorithms, useful in machine learning and signal processing contexts.

* **Ultra-Low Energy Data Recall:** Because WCFOMA can store data indefinitely and retrieve it with just a flash of light, it could serve as an archival memory that is simultaneously fast. For cold data storage (archives that are infrequently accessed), one normally uses magnetic tape or hard drives, which are slow to access. A WCFOMA-based archival storage could lie dormant with no power, then respond instantly to an optical query, reading large amounts of data with minimal energy. For instance, a WCFOMA holographic memory could store many holograms each containing a page of data, and a single laser sweep could retrieve megabytes in microseconds. This concept merges the **fast access of RAM with the non-volatility of storage**, at extremely low energy per bit (photon-level). It could be valuable in data centers aiming to reduce energy usage – data recall could be done optically rather than spinning disks or powering large DRAM banks.

* **Waveform-Authenticated Security:** WCFOMA’s reliance on specific wave interference patterns for readout can be exploited for security. One can design memory such that only a specially shaped *waveform key* will retrieve the stored data. Any other probe will yield no intelligible output (due to destructive interference or diffuse scattering). This acts as a physical unclonable function – the exact waveform (with correct phase, frequency, perhaps angle) is required to “unlock” the memory pattern. Even if an adversary knows there is data, without the precise waveform the memory stays effectively scrambled. This could protect sensitive information in hardware: for example, a security module that only responds to an optical challenge with the correct phase code. Additionally, because the ferroelectric domains are hidden inside the device, it’s resistant to traditional side-channel reading; the information is in the interference of waves, not in an easily measurable electrical state. This **waveform authentication** adds a new dimension to hardware security, potentially useful in defense, communications (e.g., encrypted optical links), and anti-tamper systems.

Each of these applications stands to benefit from WCFOMA’s *novel capabilities*: parallelism, analog compute, non-volatility, and security. As we refine the technology, we expect more applications to emerge, especially in areas merging computing with sensing (for instance, analog optical sensors that directly store what they sense as an interference pattern for later retrieval and analysis). WCFOMA represents a platform technology upon which new computational paradigms can be built, much as the invention of the memristor sparked ideas in neuromorphic circuits. The coming iterations of WCFOMA research will further quantify these benefits in real-world scenarios.

## **Conclusion**

In this paper, we presented a comprehensive vision for Wave-Coherent Ferro-Optic Memory Architecture (WCFOMA), an interdisciplinary approach that seeks to unite optical coherence and ferroelectric memory to achieve a new class of computing memory devices. We began by situating WCFOMA in the context of prior innovations, from Hopfield’s associative memory model in 1982 ( [Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC346238/#:~:text=Computational%20properties%20of%20use%20of,is%20based%20on%20asynchronous%20parallel) ) to recent advances in phase-change and ferroelectric photonic memories ( [Femtosecond structural transformation of phase-change materials far from equilibrium monitored by coherent phonons \- PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4598557/#:~:text=2,origin%20of%20the%20fast%20crystallization) ) ([Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724501/#:~:text=A%20nonvolatile%20optical%20phase%20shifter,2%7D.%20We%20observed%20a%20negative)). Building on this foundation, we proposed concrete prototyping methods (Stylus Trace and Trinary Capsule) and projected the outcomes of near-term experiments to validate the concept. Our theoretical analysis, supported by initial simulations, indicates that coherent wave interference in a suitably engineered medium can reproduce the functionality of neural-network memories and beyond, with multi-state storage and parallel readout.

The feasibility of WCFOMA is reinforced by emerging hardware demonstrations such as non-volatile ferroelectric phase shifters ([Nonvolatile optical phase shift in ferroelectric hafnium zirconium oxide \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724501/#:~:text=A%20nonvolatile%20optical%20phase%20shifter,2%7D.%20We%20observed%20a%20negative)) and optical memristors with multi-level retention ([Ferroelectric Optical Memristors Enabled by Non-Volatile Electro-Optic Effect \- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39776037/#:~:text=designed%20exclusively%20for%20the%20modulation,These%20results%20demonstrate%20a)), which we have cited to show the rapidly closing gaps in required technology. We have argued that, given these trends, the engineering challenges of WCFOMA—materials integration, cross-talk management, device scaling—are approachable with current or near-future fabrication techniques. The potential rewards, on the other hand, are substantial: orders-of-magnitude improvements in memory energy efficiency, ultra-fast associative computing operations, and novel security features, to name a few.

In conclusion, WCFOMA represents a novel and **technically plausible architecture** that pushes the frontier of memory technology from charge-based bits to wave-based patterns. By encoding information in the coherent states of light and locking those states in matter, WCFOMA blurs the line between memory and processor, digital and analog, volatile and non-volatile. The work reported here serves as a foundational step, and ongoing research will focus on scaling the prototypes, improving material performance, and ultimately building integrated systems to fully demonstrate the capabilities of coherence-based memory. We envision that WCFOMA or its derivatives could find their way into future high-performance computing hardware, optical neural network accelerators, and secure memory chips, heralding an era where **waves and ferroic matter compute together** in coherent harmony.
