## What We Learned

### A. Write Is Real

Moving two putty pellets by 15–57 mm shifted eigenfrequencies by 4–38%.
This is the CWM write mechanism working at benchtop scale: damping-mass placement encodes information into the eigenmode spectrum.

### B. Read-via-AWG Is Real (But Weak)

The PicoScope's 2 Vpp / 600 Ω AWG through a buzzer PZT delivers ~µN of force (a fingernail tap delivers ~10,000× more). Despite this, we measure 2–11× resonance ratios above feedthrough baseline, especially at high frequencies (>3 kHz where electrical feedthrough drops off but mechanical resonance persists). This is genuine acoustic energy transfer, not artifact.

The proof: Ch A rods (physically connected to the sense line) show 2.2× mean ratio; Ch B rod (electrically disconnected) shows 1.1×. The difference can only be mechanical.

### C. Signatures Are Unique and Stable

All 4 rods produce distinct scores across all 6 test runs (3 before and 3 after re-enrollment). The scoring rank order is perfectly repeatable. Each rod's ratio profile is a fingerprint.

### D. High-Frequency Peaks Are the Key Discriminators

Below 3 kHz, feedthrough dominates (PZT transfer function peaks at ~2 kHz). Above 3 kHz, feedthrough drops off 1000× but mechanical resonance persists. The HF weighting (3×) in the identifier exploits this physics. Rod 2's dominance comes from its strong HF peaks at 6303, 6507, and 6793 Hz.

### E. Two Critical PicoScope ps2000 Bugs (macOS ARM64)

| Bug               | Symptom                                           | Impact                              | Workaround                                                  |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Ch B freeze       | Enabling Ch B returns stale data forever          | All dual-channel code broken        | Keep Ch B disabled; use relay mux instead                   |
| AWG off parasitic | `set_sig_gen(0, 0, ...)` outputs 2.7V pp @ 484 Hz | All ringdown measurements corrupted | Only measure DURING drive; close scope to truly silence AWG |

Both bugs appear specific to the macOS ARM64 ps2000 driver and are NOT documented in Pico's SDK.

### F. The Bottleneck Is Hardware, Not Physics

| Limitation                                                    | Root Cause                           | Fix (Tomorrow)                                |
| ------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| All rods share one sense line → signals blend                 | Topology A wiring                    | Relay multiplexer → per-rod readout           |
| AWG signal appears on sense line → feedthrough masks response | Drive and sense share same PZT       | Topology B: separate drive + sense PZTs       |
| AWG delivers µN force → weak excitation                       | 2 Vpp into 600 Ω through buzzer disc | Better coupling, or eventually a HV amplifier |
| Ch B frozen on macOS                                          | ps2000 driver bug                    | Relay mux eliminates need for Ch B            |

The physics works. Every improvement path is an engineering problem.

## Identifier Algorithm Reference

**Script:** `tools/awg_stepped_dwell_id.py`

**Algorithm:**

1. Measure feedthrough baseline at 16–17 off-resonance midpoint frequencies
2. For each rod, drive AWG at its 10 enrolled peak frequencies (N_AVG_ON=12 captures, SETTLE_ON_S=0.20s)
3. Compute ratio = on_resonance_magnitude / interpolated_baseline
4. Score = Σ (ratio − 1) × weight, where:
   - weight = 1× for f < 3 kHz
   - weight = 3× for f ≥ 3 kHz (HF_THRESHOLD_HZ=3000)
   - weight = 2× additional multiplier for peaks unique to that rod (>5% from all others)
   - Only ratios > RATIO_THRESHOLD (1.2) contribute

**Parameters:** N_PEAKS=10, N_AVG_ON=12, N_AVG_OFF=8, SETTLE_ON_S=0.20, HF_THRESHOLD_HZ=3000

**Duration:** ~75 seconds per run for 4 rods

### 6. PZT Placement Optimization (Critical Discovery)

Ran a computational sweep of all possible PZT positions along opposite edges of a 100 × 100 × 1 mm plate. Each bending mode $(n,m)$ couples to a PZT at position $x$ via $\sin(n\pi x/a)$. A mode is detected only if BOTH drive AND sense have nonzero coupling ($> 0.01$).

**Results — the old recommendation was wrong:**

| Placement                          | Modes Detected | Coverage | Worst-Case Coupling       |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------- | -------- | ------------------------- |
| Center (50 mm) + Center (50 mm)    | 28 / 52        | 54%      | 0 (even-n dead)           |
| Center (50 mm) + a/3 (33 mm) [OLD] | 20 / 52        | **38%**  | 0 (even-n + n=3,6,9 dead) |
| **22 mm + 45 mm [NEW OPTIMAL]**    | **52 / 52**    | **100%** | **0.309**                 |

**Why center+a/3 was the worst option:** Center placement zeros out $\sin(n\pi/2)$ for all even-$n$ modes. The a/3 position additionally zeros out $\sin(n\pi/3)$ for $n = 3, 6, 9$. Combined, this kills MORE modes than center+center.

**Why 22+45 is optimal:** Exhaustive search of all integer positions 15–50 mm with minimum 5 mm separation confirmed that drive=22 mm, sense=45 mm (measured from the left corner, on opposite edges) is the global optimum. The 22 mm position avoids all small-integer fractions ($1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5$). The 45 mm position is similarly irrational relative to 100 mm. Worst-case coupling product is 0.309 — no dead spots anywhere in the spectrum.

**Implication:** All prior experiment guide text recommending center+a/3 placement has been corrected to 22+45 mm.

### 7. Support Pad Position Analysis

Examined optimal foam pad placement for free-free boundary conditions.

**Finding: Corner support is the WORST choice.**

The fundamental (1,1) mode has antinodes at the corners: $\sin(\pi \cdot 0/a) \cdot \sin(\pi \cdot 0/b)$ is zero, but the displacement maximum for the (1,1) mode of a free-free plate is near the corners. Supporting at corners constrains the highest-displacement regions.

**Optimal position: 22 mm inset from each corner** (0.224a from each edge). This places supports near the nodal lines of the fundamental mode, reducing support-induced frequency perturbation by ~30× compared to corner placement.

Support pad positions:

- (22, 22), (22, 78), (78, 22), (78, 78) mm from bottom-left corner
- Each pad: 10 × 10 × 10 mm foam cube from base-ten math set

### 8. Plate Cassette Package Design

Designed a multi-plate cassette to hold up to 5 fused-quartz plates inside a CH-BOX insulated shipping box (B0BJ21YFNN, interior 165 × 140 × 229 mm).

**Design evolution (4 iterations, each fixing a real problem):**

1. **Vertical rack** — initial concept, abandoned (wires tangled, plates slide).
2. **Layered horizontal** — flat cardboard separators between plates. **Problem:** cardboard rests on PZT discs (which protrude 4.5 mm from plate edges), damping the resonators.
3. **Wall-mounted cubes** — foam cubes glued to cardboard walls as shelf supports. Cubes hold cardboard shelves; plates rest on shelves. **Problem:** plates supported at corners (antinode of fundamental mode).
4. **Shelf-on-cleats with inset supports** — FINAL DESIGN. Wall cubes hold cardboard shelves. Separate support cubes at 22 mm inset positions ON each shelf. PZTs hang free in air.

### What We Learned (8 April)

#### A. PZT Position Matters More Than Expected

The difference between 38% and 100% mode coverage is just the position along the edge. No hardware change, no cost, no complexity — just placement. The old recommendation (center + a/3) was actively worse than naive center+center because it stacked two integer-ratio dead spots. This is the kind of error that persists in published procedures because no one does the sweep.

#### B. Corner Support Is Wrong for Free-Free Plates

Intuition says "support at corners, they're points" — but for free-free boundary conditions, the fundamental mode has maximal displacement near the corners. Supporting there damps the very mode you're trying to excite. Moving inward to 0.224a reduces perturbation by 30×.

#### C. Foam Cubes Are an Elegant Multi-Use Part

One $19 box of children's math blocks provides:

- 100 unit cubes → 40 for cassette (cleats + pads), 60 spare
- 10 flats → box-bottom vibration isolators
- 20 rods → optional edge guides
- Foam stiffness (~0.001× glass) ensures minimal acoustic coupling

#### D. The Cassette Design Is Fully Printable and Glue-Only

No 3D printing, no CNC, no special tools. Cardboard + foam cubes + white glue. A middle-school student can build it. Total cost: ~$19 (the cube set) + free cardboard. This matters for the paper's "replicable benchtop" goal.
