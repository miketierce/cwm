### 2. Cymatics–Script Spectral Correlation Experiment

#### Motivation

Test whether the earliest Hebrew and Sanskrit letterforms were "observed interference patterns" — a claim from the cymatics literature (Jenny, 1967) that has never been subjected to rigorous spectral analysis. The key constraint: **all analysis must be sonic/spectral, not visual comparison**.

#### Pipeline Design

```
glyph image → 2D FFT → dominant spatial frequencies (kx, ky)
    → map to plate eigenmode indices (n, m)
        → eigenfrequencies f_nm (Kirchhoff theory)
            → census sweep magnitude lookup
                → acoustic fingerprint per glyph
                    → discriminability test vs null controls
```

#### Implementation

Created `simulations/cymatics_spectral.py` (~1100 lines) implementing four hypotheses:

- **H-CYM1:** Do script glyphs map to distinguishable acoustic fingerprints? (template matching accuracy > 90%)
- **H-CYM2:** Do real scripts produce higher spectral distance than complexity-matched random shapes? (permutation test, α = 0.05)
- **H-CYM3:** Does projecting from a higher-dimensional cavity (3D–11D) improve glyph discriminability over the 2D baseline? (sweep 2D through 11D)
- **H-CYM4:** Do phonetically paired Hebrew–Sanskrit letters (beth↔ba, shin↔sha, etc.) produce more similar acoustic fingerprints than random cross-script pairs? (separation > 2σ)

#### The Higher-Dimensional Projection Mathematics

For an N-dimensional rectangular cavity with sides $(a_1, \ldots, a_N)$, eigenmodes are:

$$\phi_{n_1 \ldots n_N} = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \sin\!\left(\frac{n_i \pi x_i}{a_i}\right)$$

Projecting onto the 2D observation plane $(x_1, x_2)$ by integrating out dimensions 3 through N:

$$\int_0^{a_i} \sin\!\left(\frac{n_i \pi x_i}{a_i}\right) dx_i = \begin{cases} \frac{2a_i}{n_i\pi} & n_i \text{ odd} \\ 0 & n_i \text{ even}\end{cases}$$

Each surviving N-dimensional mode projects onto a 2D mode $(n_1, n_2)$ with weight proportional to $\prod_{i=3}^{N} 1/n_i$ (only odd extra-dimensional indices survive). This is standard separable PDE / Fourier analysis — no speculative physics.

## Literature Review: Festi et al. (2026), Phys. Rev. X 16, 021021

**"Effect of Glass Stability on the Low Frequency Vibrations of Vapor Deposited Glasses"**
I. Festi, E. Alfinelli, D. Bessas, F. Caporaletti, A. I. Chumakov, M. Moratalla, M. A. Ramos, M. Rodríguez-López, C. Rodríguez-Tinoco, J. Rodríguez-Viejo, and G. Baldi.
Published 28 April 2026.

### What They Did

Used a novel nuclear resonant x-ray spectrograph (NRAIXS) at the ESRF to measure the density of vibrational states (DOS) of ultrastable (USG) and ordinary (OG) vapor-deposited TPD organic glasses down to 70 GHz — over a decade below the boson peak (BP). Unprecedented 30 GHz (~130 µeV) frequency resolution. Compared DOS against two theoretical models: the soft potential model (SPM) and heterogeneous elasticity theory (HET).

### Key Results

1. **Glass stability does NOT affect low-frequency harmonic modes.** The excess coefficient $A_{\text{ex}}$ (the $\nu^4$ departure from the Debye level) is identical within error for USG and OG ($0.65 \pm 0.03$ vs $0.66 \pm 0.06$ THz⁻⁵), despite a 5× reduction in two-level states (TLS).

2. **The boson peak IS sensitive to stability.** BP intensity drops by ~50% from OG to USG and shifts to slightly higher frequency. This is consistent with numerical simulations of vapor-deposited glasses.

3. **Quasilocalized modes (QLM) are subdominant.** The measured $A_{\text{ex}}$ is an order of magnitude larger than what QLM alone can explain. The dominant mechanism for the $\nu^4$ excess is **Rayleigh scattering of sound waves**, not localized defect modes.

4. **The Ioffe-Regel limit** ($\nu_{\text{IR}} \approx 0.36$ THz for TPD) coincides with the BP position — above this frequency, modes transition from propagating to diffusive.

5. **HET (heterogeneous elasticity theory) works for the OG but fails for the USG** — it predicts a BP intensity 2× the Debye level, but the USG excess is only 50%.

### Relevance to CWM — Quantitative Analysis

**Operating regime comparison:**

| Parameter | Festi (TPD) | CWM (fused silica) | Ratio |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|-------|
| $\nu_D$ (Debye freq) | 4.27 THz | 10.38 THz | 2.4× |
| $\nu_{\text{BP}}$ (Boson peak) | ~0.3 THz | ~1.0 THz | 3.3× |
| $\nu_{\text{IR}}$ (Ioffe-Regel) | 0.36 THz | ~1.0 THz | 2.8× |
| $v_L$ | 3022 m/s | 5960 m/s | 2.0× |
| $v_T$ | 1392 m/s | 3760 m/s | 2.7× |
| $\rho$ | 1050 kg/m³ | 2200 kg/m³ | 2.1× |
| Our max operating freq | — | 100 kHz = 10⁻⁷ THz | — |

**CWM operates $10^7\times$ below the Ioffe-Regel limit.** Every mode on our plates is a clean, propagating, harmonic wave. No anharmonicity, no TLS effects, no Rayleigh broadening.

#### 1. Rayleigh Scattering Is Irrelevant at Our Frequencies

Sound attenuation follows $\Gamma \sim A_R \nu^4$ (Rayleigh scattering law). Using THz measurements on vitreous SiO₂ (Baldi 2010, 2014; Wang 2025):

- At 1 THz: $\Gamma \approx 10$ GHz
- At 100 kHz: $\Gamma \approx 10^{-18}$ Hz → $\tau \approx 10^{18}$ s (31 billion years)
- $Q_{\text{Rayleigh}} = \pi f / \Gamma \approx 3 \times 10^{23}$

Rayleigh scattering contributes **zero** loss at our frequencies. This is a $\nu^4$ scaling — going from 1 THz to 100 kHz is a factor of $10^{28}$ reduction in attenuation.

#### 2. Q-Factor Diagnosis

The Festi paper helps us triangulate our Q-factor measurements:

| Loss mechanism | Q contribution | Bottleneck? |
|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| Rayleigh scattering | $3 \times 10^{23}$ | No |
| Thermoelastic (TED) | $3.95 \times 10^{7}$ | No |
| Material (intrinsic) | $10^5$ (fused silica) | **Ceiling** |
| Measured (ringdown) | $7{,}687 - 33{,}960$ | Actual |

The gap between material ceiling ($10^5$) and measured ($10^{3.9}–10^{4.5}$) is entirely explained by **extrinsic losses**: PZT/epoxy mounting, edge support, and air damping. This means:
- The glass itself is not the problem. The signal chain is.
- The preamp won't improve Q — it will improve SNR to *measure* the existing Q more accurately.
- Going to vacuum would improve Q. Going to better mounting (e.g., nodal point support) would improve it more.

#### 3. Two-Level States Are Irrelevant at Room Temperature

Festi's key finding — TLS density reduced by 5× in USG but harmonic modes unchanged — is academic for us. TLS contribute a $C_{\text{TLS}} \cdot T$ term to specific heat that dominates only below ~10 K. At 300 K, TLS is negligible compared to phonon bath losses. **However**, for space/cryogenic CWM applications (Starlink, Mars), TLS becomes the dominant loss mechanism, and ultrastable glass substrates (vapor-deposited at $0.85 T_g$) would be the path to even higher Q.

#### 4. Heterogeneous Elasticity Validates PUF Uniqueness

The HET disorder parameter $\gamma \approx 0.3$ for fused silica (Pan et al. 2021, Ref [47]) means local shear modulus fluctuates by ~30% on nanoscale. This is the **condensed matter physics basis** for our plate fingerprinting claim. Each plate's eigenmode spectrum is determined by its unique spatial distribution of elastic heterogeneity — a physical unclonable function grounded in glass structure, not just "manufacturing variation."

This is stronger language than we've used in the paper. We can cite Schirmacher's HET theory directly.

#### 5. Implications for MEMS-Scale CWM

When we shrink to 1 mm rods/plates with modes in MHz–GHz range, the Festi physics becomes directly relevant:

- At 1 GHz ($10^{-3}$ THz): still $10^3\times$ below Ioffe-Regel, but Rayleigh attenuation scales as $\nu^4$
- $\Gamma_{1\text{GHz}} \approx A_R \cdot (10^9)^4 = 10^{-2}$ Hz → $Q_{\text{Rayleigh}} \approx 3 \times 10^{11}$ (still not the bottleneck)
- At 50 GHz: $\Gamma \approx 6 \times 10^4$ Hz → $Q \approx 3 \times 10^6$ — now comparable to material Q for high-purity silica
- **Conclusion**: Rayleigh scattering becomes the Q limiter only above ~10 GHz for fused silica. Below that, material and anchor losses dominate.

## Free-Hanging Plate Experiment Design

### Motivation

Festi et al. proves bulk fused silica is perfectly harmonic at our frequencies. Therefore, any nonlinearity we observe (intermod products, THD) must originate from:
1. **Contact/boundary nonlinearity** — PZT epoxy bond, clamping points
2. **Geometric nonlinearity** — large-displacement plate bending
3. **Air-loading nonlinearity** — acoustic radiation coupling

A free-hanging plate eliminates (1) and greatly reduces (3), isolating geometric nonlinearity. This is the cleanest test of intrinsic plate nonlinear response.

### Practical Setup

**Materials needed (all on hand):**
- 1× fused silica plate (100 mm × 100 mm × 1 mm)
- 2× lengths of thin nylon monofilament (fishing line, 0.1–0.2 mm dia)
- 1× rigid frame (lab stand + crossbar, or simply two tall items on the bench)
- 2× PZT transducers (same ones used currently)
- Cyanoacrylate (superglue) or wax dots for temporary PZT attachment
- PicoScope + relay mux (existing)

**Suspension method:**
```
       ┌─────────── crossbar ───────────┐
       │                                │
       │   ● nylon loop          nylon  │
       │   │                    loop ●  │
       │   │                         │  │
       ├───┤                         ├──┤
       │   ▼                         ▼  │
       │  ╔══════════════════════════╗   │
       │  ║                          ║   │
       │  ║    fused silica plate    ║   │
       │  ║         100×100×1 mm     ║   │
       │  ║                          ║   │
       │  ║  PZT(drive) ●    ● PZT  ║   │
       │  ║             (sense)      ║   │
       │  ╚══════════════════════════╝   │
       └────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Suspension points:** The key insight is where to hang the plate. For a square plate, the **nodal lines** of the lowest modes cross at specific points. The (1,1) fundamental has antinodes at centre and corners, with nodal lines at approximately 22% from each edge. Hanging from two points along one edge at ~22 mm from each corner minimizes damping of the lowest modes.

**Alternative: 4-point cradle.** Loop fishing line under all four edges to form a "hammock." This supports the plate at its perimeter while leaving both faces free. Slightly higher air damping from the cradle but mechanically simpler.

**PZT attachment options (ranked by Q impact):**
1. **Best: wax dots** (beeswax or paraffin) — removable, minimal coupling area, easy cleanup. Apply a ~2 mm wax dot, press PZT onto plate, let set 30s. Q impact: minimal.
2. **Good: double-sided tape** (Kapton preferred) — removable, thin bond line. Q impact: small.
3. **Acceptable: cyanoacrylate** — strong bond but permanent and brittle. Q impact: moderate (introduces a stiff boundary condition at the bond area).
4. **Avoid: epoxy** — our current method. Thick bond layer, high damping, hard to remove. Q impact: significant — this is likely a major contributor to our extrinsic Q loss.

### Test Protocol

**Test A: Free-hanging Q measurement**
1. Suspend plate, attach PZTs with wax dots
2. Run existing census sweep (200 Hz – 100 kHz, 25 Hz steps)
3. Compare eigenmode count and peak widths vs rigid-mounted plate
4. Compute Q from 3 dB bandwidth on strongest peaks
5. **Expected:** Q increases 2–10× over rigid mount. If Q > 30,000, we're approaching the material ceiling.

**Test B: Free-hanging intermod (THD test)**
1. Same suspension
2. Drive plate with AWG at two frequencies (e.g., 29.3 kHz + 41.7 kHz)
3. Measure at IM2 (f1+f2, f2-f1) and IM3 (2f1-f2, 2f2-f1) frequencies
4. Compare IM product amplitudes vs rigid-mounted plate
5. **If IM products disappear:** nonlinearity was contact/boundary (PZT-epoxy-glass interface)
6. **If IM products persist at same level:** nonlinearity is geometric (large-displacement bending)
7. **If IM products reduce but don't disappear:** both mechanisms contribute

**Test C: Drive-level sweep (geometric nonlinearity threshold)**
1. Sweep AWG amplitude: 100 mVpp, 200, 500, 1000, 2000, 4000 mVpp
2. At each level, measure IM2 and IM3 products
3. **Geometric nonlinearity signature:** IM2 scales as $V_{\text{drive}}^2$, IM3 as $V_{\text{drive}}^3$
4. **Contact nonlinearity signature:** IM products appear above a threshold and scale erratically
5. This characterizes the nonlinear capacity of the free plate — needed for CIM scaling

### How Free-Hanging Translates to MEMS

The free-hanging plate experiment directly maps to MEMS resonator design. Here is the translation table:

| Macro (free-hanging plate) | MEMS equivalent | Design parameter |
|---------------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Nylon thread suspension** | **Tether anchors at nodal points** | Tether placement along nodal lines minimizes anchor loss, exactly as suspending from nodal points minimizes thread damping |
| **Wax-dot PZT coupling** | **Thin-film AlN piezo** | Minimal coupling area, low acoustic impedance mismatch. Wax dot ≈ MEMS thin-film: both are low-mass, compliant bonds |
| **Air gap (no rigid mount)** | **Released MEMS (undercut etch)** | The HF/BOE undercut that releases a MEMS plate from its substrate is the manufacturing equivalent of lifting the plate off the bench |
| **Free-plate Q measurement** | **Anchor loss calibration** | Free-plate Q isolates material + air losses. Difference from tethered Q = anchor loss. This maps directly to `compute_Q_anchor()` |
| **IM product measurement** | **Nonlinear capacity characterization** | If the free plate shows geometric nonlinearity, MEMS plates of the same material/thickness will too — it's a material property, not a packaging artifact |
| **4-point cradle** | **Perimeter-anchored membrane** | A plate supported at its perimeter with a released center is the standard MEMS drumhead topology |

### MEMS Anchor Design Implications

The free-hanging experiment answers a critical MEMS question: **how much Q do we lose to anchoring?**

Current model (`mems_q_model.py`) uses the Hao/Photiadis/Judge formulas for anchor loss in beams. For plates, the key parameter is the **tether-to-plate area ratio** and **tether placement relative to mode nodal lines**:

- **Tethers at antinodes:** maximum anchor loss. Like clamping the plate at its corners.
- **Tethers at nodal lines:** minimum anchor loss. Like suspending from the 22% points.
- **Perimeter anchoring (drumhead):** moderate loss. Like the 4-point cradle.

The free-hanging test gives us the **anchor-free baseline Q**. Then:

$$Q_{\text{anchor}} = \left(\frac{1}{Q_{\text{tethered}}} - \frac{1}{Q_{\text{free}}}\right)^{-1}$$

This directly calibrates the MEMS anchor loss model without any simulation assumptions.
