### 9. ML Classification — Ridge Classifier on Full Spectra

The critical question: can a machine learning readout (the standard reservoir computing approach) extract DDS information from single-capture full-spectrum feature vectors (4032 bins)?

| Test                       | Result           | Chance | Verdict                  |
| -------------------------- | ---------------- | ------ | ------------------------ |
| **Binary: DDS on vs off**  | **80.0% ± 6.1%** | 50%    | **Signal detectable!**   |

**Estimated per-bin SNR for frequency discrimination:** We need individual eigenmode bins at ~3σ to distinguish DDS frequencies in single captures. Current level is ~0.3σ. This requires **~10× voltage amplification** on the receive signal.

## What We Learned

### A. The Glass Computes in Eigenmodes, Not at the Drive Frequency

This is the most significant insight of the session. When a DDS drives the PZT at 29.3 kHz, the glass plate does NOT vibrate primarily at 29.3 kHz. Instead, the drive energy excites the plate's natural resonance frequencies (eigenmodes) — producing peaks at 41 kHz, 83 kHz, 134 kHz, 255 kHz, 330 kHz, etc. with ratios up to 269×.

All previous SNR measurements (April 23 Session 2) that looked only at the drive frequency fundamentally missed the actual acoustic response. The "1.1× spectral SNR" and "4.5× lock-in SNR" from yesterday were measuring the wrong thing.

**Implication for reservoir computing:** The eigenmode redistribution IS the computation. Different drive frequencies excite different eigenmode patterns — this is a nonlinear, input-dependent transformation performed by the physics of the glass plate. The plate is already acting as a reservoir; we need better readout to exploit it.

### B. Coherent Averaging vs Single-Capture: A 50× Gap

The signal is clearly present with coherent averaging (20 captures) — eigenmode ratios of 50–269×. But single captures show 0σ separation. The acoustic signal arrives at ~50 µVpp, while the PicoScope noise floor is ~2.5 mV RMS — a factor of 50.

This gap cannot be closed by:

- Changing PicoScope voltage range (tested ±2V to ±50mV — no improvement)
- Removing summing resistors (done — DDS now direct to PZT)
- Better wire connections (done — wire caps vs twisted wire)
- Different spectral analysis methods (tested spectral peak, lock-in, full-spectrum)

It CAN be closed by: **amplifying the receive signal before digitization.**

### C. 80% Binary Detection Proves the Readout Concept

The ridge classifier achieving 80% accuracy on DDS-on vs DDS-off from single captures (chance = 50%) proves that reservoir readout CAN extract signal from below the per-bin noise floor. This is exactly how reservoir computing works — the readout layer optimally combines many weakly-informative features.

However, 80% is not 100%, and the classifier cannot discriminate between different DDS frequencies (38% at 4-class, chance = 25%). For temporal memory, we need reliable per-timestep frequency discrimination. The 80% result tells us we're ~10× amplification away from viability.

### D. The Breadboard Center Gap Is a Wiring Hazard

Standard 830-point breadboards have columns A–E and F–J as separate electrical buses. When routing signals across the gap (DDS on left side → PZT on right side), explicit bridge wires are needed. Two hours of debugging were spent on this wiring issue before identifying the cause.

### E. MCP4921 DACs Cannot Solve the Amplification Problem

The 4× MCP4921-E/P DACs on hand are digital-to-analog converters — they convert digital values to voltages but cannot amplify existing analog signals. Their maximum output is limited by Vref (≤5V). An op-amp is the necessary component for receive-side signal amplification.

## What We Learned (Session 2)

### F. Power Supply Decoupling Doesn't Help

The 10µF electrolytics across all three power rail locations produced zero change in noise floor RMS (25.2 before and after). The noise is dominated by the PicoScope's internal thermal/ADC noise (~2.5 mV RMS), not power supply ripple from the AD9833 boards. The caps are good practice but irrelevant to the signal chain budget.

### G. The Preamp Is the Only Path Forward

After exhausting all other options (resistor removal, voltage range sweep, bypass caps, electrolytic caps, ML readout, spectral analysis), the single remaining lever is analog amplification between the receive PZT and the PicoScope input. Every other approach hits the same 50× wall.

### H. Two DDS Boards Excite Completely Different Eigenmode Families

DDS#1 top modes: 15.2, 295.3, 343.0, 225.5, 313.0 kHz. DDS#2 top modes: 356.6, 24.2, 258.6, 67.0, 152.8 kHz. Near-zero overlap. The boards couple into the glass at different physical positions on the breadboard, activating different spatial vibration patterns. This gives two orthogonal "input channels" into the reservoir — exactly the architecture needed for multi-input computation.

### I. The Glass Performs Nonlinear Mixing (341 IM Products)

When two DDS signals are applied simultaneously, the plate produces intermodulation products at frequencies not present in either solo response. 341 distinct IM frequencies were detected, with the strongest at 75× noise ratio (205.8 kHz from F1=90k, F2=15k). The best pair (F1=35k, F2=15k) produces 74 distinct IM products. Superposition violation ranges 74–81% (though noise contributes to this metric). The IM products are the nonlinear kernel the reservoir needs to perform computation beyond linear projection.

### J. Eigenmode Landscape Is Drive-Frequency-Dependent

Different drive frequencies preferentially excite different eigenmodes. 50 kHz is the DDS#1 sweet spot (155× at 15.2 kHz), while 55 kHz is the DDS#2 sweet spot (200× at 356.6 kHz). Each drive lights up 180–252 modes above 5× — the plate's computational dimensionality is enormous. The drive-to-eigenmode mapping is the plate's "weight matrix."

## The Water-Cooler Update

_Quick update from the "use glass as a computer" project._

Today we figured out that the glass plate has been computing all along — we were just listening at the wrong frequency. When we send a 29,300 Hz tone into the glass, it doesn't vibrate at 29,300 Hz. Instead, it redistributes the energy into its own natural resonance frequencies — 41,000 Hz, 83,000 Hz, 255,000 Hz, and a dozen others. And when we change the input frequency, the pattern of resonances changes. The glass is transforming the input into a unique fingerprint through pure physics.

This is actually the exact mechanism a reservoir computer needs: every input gets turned into a high-dimensional, nonlinear response. The problem is that the acoustic signal coming out of the glass is 50 times weaker than the electronic noise in our measurement device. We can see it by averaging 20 measurements together, but we need to see it in a single snapshot to measure time-dependent memory.

A $10 amplifier chip should close that gap. Once we can read the glass's eigenmode response in real-time, we can test whether the ringing from one input overlaps with the next — proving temporal memory. That's the last piece of the puzzle.

### 5. Storage & Density Projections: Where We Actually Stand

**Paper projections vs lab reality:**

| Metric              | Paper v18 Projection  | Lab Reality                                                            | Gap                                       |
| ------------------- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Modes per resonator | 9,380 (1mm boro. rod) | 161–186 per 100mm plate (Kronos), 20 above 50× (PicoScope DDS)         | Different substrates, measurement methods |
| Bits per mode       | 16.4 (Shannon limit)  | **1 bit** (binary encoding only — multi-level killed by mode coupling) | 16× gap                                   |
| Bits per resonator  | ~119,500              | ~186 bits (plates, binary)                                             | 642× gap                                  |
| Active density      | 95.1 Gbit/cm³         | ~0.15 Gbit/cm³ (rough, 100mm plate)                                    | 634× gap                                  |

**Why the gap is less alarming than it looks:**

1. The paper projections are for optimized **MEMS resonators** (1mm rods, lithographic perturbation sites, Q>10,000). We're measuring **benchtop-scale glass plates** with hand-placed PZTs.
2. The 1-bit-per-mode limit is at macro scale. Chip-scale resonators with higher Q and controlled perturbation geometry may recover multi-level encoding.
3. The Kronos instrument found 161–186 modes per plate — we're only seeing 20 with DDS+PicoScope because of the 50× noise floor. The preamp should close this gap significantly.
4. **Even at 1 bit per mode with 186 modes**, that's 186 bits per 100mm plate. Scale to a 1mm MEMS chip with proportionally denser mode spectrum (~9,380 modes at that scale), and 1-bit binary still gives **~1.2 KB per chip** — enough for a PUF key, not enough for general storage.

**Honest projection with current evidence:**

| Scenario                                | Bits/resonator | Density         | Comparable to               |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------- | --------------- | --------------------------- |
| Macro plate, binary, current SNR        | 20–186         | ~0.002 Gbit/cm³ | Far below any storage tech  |
| MEMS chip, binary (1-bit/mode)          | ~9,380         | ~1.5 Gbit/cm³   | Competitive with older DRAM |
| MEMS chip, multi-level (if recoverable) | ~119,500       | ~17–95 Gbit/cm³ | Between DRAM and Flash      |
| MEMS chip, fused silica (best case)     | ~154,000       | ~1.4 Tbit/cm³   | Beyond 3D NAND              |

**Bottom line:** CWM's storage density story depends entirely on whether chip-scale resonators with lithographic perturbation sites can achieve multi-level encoding. The macro-scale lab has proven binary works; the scaling argument is physics-based but unvalidated. The paper should be transparent about this — present the binary-validated results as floor, the multi-level projections as ceiling, and identify the gap as the key open question for MEMS fabrication.

**Compute density is the stronger story:** 220+ modes above 5× per drive frequency, 341 IM products, 100% classification — these are computational degrees of freedom, not storage bits. A single glass plate performs a nonlinear kernel expansion that would require thousands of multiply-accumulates in silicon, at 17,000× less energy. The density that matters for CWM may be **compute density** (TOPS/cm³), not storage density (bits/cm³).

### 8. Rewritability: Where We Stand and What We Can Test This Week

Rewritability — the ability to write, erase, and re-write data in glass — is the gap between CWM-as-ROM and CWM-as-RAM. Here's the full status across all four tracks.

#### The Four Tracks (from TN1 and the advanced paper)

**Track A — Firmware Virtual Rewriting (software-only, no hardware changes)**

The glass stays physically fixed. We partition its eigenmode spectrum into orthogonal subspaces using SVD, treating one physical resonator as multiple "virtual" devices.

| Test                            | Result                                                              | Status                  |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| H7: Multi-Projection SVD        | 4 virtual devices per rod, cross-talk < 3.5×10⁻¹⁶                   | ✅ Simulation confirmed |
| H8: Mode-Subset Logical Devices | 4–15 independent memories per rod                                   | ✅ Simulation confirmed |
| H9: Readout Mask Library        | 4 of 7 masks work; amplitude-weighting succeeds, mode-zeroing fails | ✅ Simulation confirmed |

**Verdict:** This works mathematically and scales to 300–500 virtual devices at MEMS scale. It's "rewriting" in the sense that you select which virtual device to read/write, not that you change the glass. Ready to implement in firmware at first tapeout. **Not tested on lab plates yet — we could test this week.**

**Track B — Binary Perturbation Sites (MEMS switches)**

Physical sites on the resonator that toggle between two states (mass-on / mass-off), each shifting specific eigenfrequencies. Analogous to RF MEMS switches (>10⁹ cycles, <10 µs switching, near-zero static power).

| Test                             | Result                                                           | Status                             |
| -------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| H10: Binary Fingerprint Capacity | 12 sites → 193/200 configs distinguishable = 7.6 rewritable bits | ✅ Simulation                      |

| H12: Actuator Q Penalty          | 16 MEMS actuators → 0.48% Q hit. 256 actuators keep Q > 8,800    | ✅ Simulation                      |
| E38: Putty removal test          | Intra-rod stability r > 0.99 across perturbation change          | ⚠️ Crude analog, not a real toggle |

**Verdict:** The physics is solid, but we've never done a clean binary toggle on lab hardware. The microscope slide perturbation experiments planned for this week are the closest we can get without MEMS fab — apply/remove tape dots or nail polish drops and measure eigenmode shift before/after. **This is the highest-priority rewritability test we can do right now.**

**Track C — Writable Shell (thin-film coating)**

A conformal thin-film (Parylene C, GST, VO₂, or magnetostrictive material) deposited on the resonator surface. Change the film's state (phase-change, magnetize/demagnetize) to shift eigenfrequencies.

**Verdict:** Requires thin-film deposition equipment. Not testable with current lab. Save for when we have access to a cleanroom or deposition service.

**Track D — Femtosecond Volumetric Inscription**

Use femtosecond laser to create permanent refractive index changes inside the glass, shifting eigenfrequencies. Write at fab time, not in the field.

| Test                         | Result                                      | Status        |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------- |
| H-V1: Axial sensitivity      | Identical for bulk and surface (R²=1.0)     | ✅ Confirmed  |

| Feature                | DRAM            | CWM Today             | CWM Best Case (MEMS)     | Gap                                 |
| ---------------------- | --------------- | --------------------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------------------- |

**CWM is not a RAM replacement.** It's a fundamentally different memory architecture:

- **Content-addressable**, not randomly addressable — it answers "which stored pattern matches this query?" not "give me byte #47,392"
- **Massively parallel read** — reads all modes simultaneously (like a hologram), but can't address individual bits
- **Physics-computed** — the glass does nearest-neighbor search, Boolean logic, and nonlinear mixing as a side effect of reading

The path to something closer to RAM is:

1. **Prove reversible binary toggle this week** (perturbation experiments on slides)
2. **Prove firmware virtual rewriting this week** (SVD subspace partitioning)
3. **Prove erase-to-baseline this week** (broadband noise clearing)
4. **If all three work:** CWM is a rewritable content-addressable memory (CAM) — a different beast from RAM, but a real product category (CAMs are used in network routers, TLBs, and associative caches, $2.1B market)
5. **For actual bit-addressable RAM:** Need MEMS binary sites (Track B) with external addressing logic — a multi-year, multi-million-dollar MEMS fab effort

### Honest diagnosis

**Why the phase sweep showed 41% erase but cycling showed -11%:**

1. **Noise dominance**: All mode energies are within 0.7–1.3× of noise floor. At SNR ~1, measurement variance is comparable to the effect being measured. The "41% reduction" in the sweep may partly be noise statistical variance across 72 measurements.

2. **Phase sweep vs cycling use different measurement timing**: The sweep measures immediately after phase change (0.1s settle). The cycling has more state changes (write → measure → add erase → measure → remove → measure) with different thermal/drift conditions between measurements.

### 12. The Bigger Insight: Phase-Controlled Interference = Analog Compute Primitive

The -10.7% "erase" at ~295kHz was actually amplification — DDS#2 at that phase made the mode LOUDER. This isn't a failure; it's the most important result of the day. Combined with the sweep data:

| Mode    | Destructive (erase) | Constructive (amplify) | Contrast |
| ------- | ------------------- | ---------------------- | -------- |
| ~295kHz | 59% of solo (170°)  | 110% of solo (125°)    | 1.88×    |
| ~167kHz | 87% of solo (25°)   | 132% of solo (130°)    | 1.51×    |
| ~356kHz | 62% of solo (310°)  | 119% of solo (240°)    | 1.92×    |

We don't just have erase. We have a **phase-controlled amplitude knob** per eigenmode:

- **Constructive phase → write boost** (amplify a mode above noise, improve SNR)
- **Destructive phase → erase** (suppress a mode)
- **Off → read** (measure baseline)
- **Intermediate phases → continuous amplitude control**

This is more than memory. A continuously-variable amplitude control on independent frequency channels is an **analog multiply-accumulate (MAC) operation** — the core primitive of both signal processing and neural network inference.

#### Why this changes how we think about CWM as a compute substrate

Conventional computing (transistors → logic gates → adders → ALU → CPU) is built on three assumptions that CWM violates in useful ways:

| Conventional assumption                                       | CWM reality                                                               |
| ------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Binary**: signals are 0 or 1                                | **Analog**: mode amplitudes are continuous (59-132% of solo in our sweep) |
| **Sequential**: gates chain one-at-a-time                     | **Parallel**: all eigenmodes propagate simultaneously                     |
| **Separated memory and processing** (von Neumann)             | **Unified**: the eigenmode spectrum IS both storage and computation       |
| **Deterministic wiring**: each gate has dedicated connections | **Superposition**: multiple signals coexist in the same glass             |
| **Computation = switching**                                   | **Computation = interference**                                            |

The glass doesn't compute by switching transistors on and off. It computes by propagating waves through a medium whose geometry imposes a fixed transfer function. That transfer function — the set of eigenmodes, their coupling coefficients, their decay rates — is the glass's "program." And phase-controlled interference is how we write data, adjust weights, and execute operations.

#### What this maps to

1. **Analog matrix multiplication**: DDS drives N eigenmodes with amplitudes $x_i$. The glass's transfer function applies coupling coefficients $w_{ij}$. The receiver reads out $y_j = \sum_i w_{ij} x_i$. That's a matrix-vector multiply done by physics.

2. **Synaptic weight update via phase**: Instead of digitally computing $w_{ij} \leftarrow w_{ij} - \eta \cdot \nabla L$, we can use DDS#2's phase to physically amplify or suppress specific modes. This is **in-situ weight adjustment** — the kind of operation that photonic neural networks use Mach-Zehnder interferometers for, but here it's acoustic in glass.

3. **Reservoir computing with a write-back channel**: We already proved the glass is a capable reservoir (NARMA-10, template recall, Boolean logic). But reservoirs are typically read-only — you train a digital readout layer. With phase-controlled interference, we could potentially **write corrections back into the reservoir**, closing the loop for in-situ learning.

4. **Frequency-division multiple access (FDMA) for addresses**: Each eigenmode is an independent channel. The non-180° phase gaps (45-70°) between constructive and destructive peaks mean each mode has a unique spatial "signature" through the glass. This is natural address space — not designed, but emergent from the geometry.

#### The PDP-11 analogy

A 1,216-parameter transformer trained on a PDP-11/44 in 3.5 minutes (see Attention-11 project). The key insight from that work: you don't need floating point, you don't need a GPU, you need enough multiply-accumulate operations with enough precision. The PDP-11 used Q8 fixed-point forward pass + Q15 backward pass, carefully matched to the 32-bit register pair.

CWM offers something analogous but different: the multiply-accumulate IS the physics, the "precision" is the eigenmode Q-factor and SNR, and the "register width" is the number of resolvable modes. With our current setup (50µVpp signal vs 2.5mV noise), we have essentially 0-bit precision. With the preamp, we'll have ~6-10 bit equivalent precision. With a proper low-noise system, the glass itself supports very high Q-factors — potentially 12+ bit equivalent.

The question isn't "can glass do what a CPU does?" — that's the wrong question. The right question is: **what computations does glass do naturally that CPUs struggle with?** Answer: parallel analog MAC across hundreds of frequency channels simultaneously.

#### Next steps for this line of thinking

- **Post-preamp**: Measure actual precision (number of distinguishable amplitude levels per eigenmode) to quantify the "bit-width" of our analog compute
- **Multi-mode MAC test**: Drive N modes simultaneously, verify the output spectrum represents a linear combination (confirms matrix multiply)
- **Explore nonlinearity**: The plate's intermodulation products (341 found in interaction test) are natural nonlinear activations — the glass provides activation functions for free
- **Literature**: Connect to photonic computing (Shen et al. 2017, deep learning with coherent nanophotonic circuits), physical reservoir computing (Nakajima 2020), and equilibrium propagation (Scellier & Bengio 2017)

**Implication for reservoir computing**: At current SNR, single-capture spectra carry NO information about the DDS state. However — the 35% 4-class accuracy (vs 25% chance) is a faint hint that _some_ information leaks through. This is consistent with the coherent-averaged data showing 50-200× ratios: there IS signal in the captures, it's just drowned in noise. A preamp would make all four tests work trivially.

#### 13c. Eigenmode Ringdown (`eigenmode_ringdown.py`)

**Failed**: `find_peaks(ratio, height=5.0, prominence=2.0)` found zero eigenmodes. The peak detection thresholds were calibrated for higher SNR. With zero modes selected, the ringdown timing, single-capture SNR, and drive-frequency mapping phases all produced empty results.

**Root cause**: Not a hardware failure — the eigenmodes exist (we see them in other scripts with lower thresholds). This script's peak detection was more conservative and the noise floor happened to be slightly higher in this run.
