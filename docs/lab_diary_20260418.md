## What We Learned

### A. Cross-Detection Nearly Doubles the Usable Mode Count

The original census found 63 modes by sweeping each receiver independently and peak-detecting its self-response. Cross-detection found 118 additional modes by checking whether a receiver responds to frequencies "owned" by other plates. The physics: AWG broadcasts to ALL plates simultaneously, so every plate vibrates at every frequency — but with plate-specific amplitudes determined by their individual mode structures. These crosstalk responses are perfectly valid Boolean features because they still discriminate which plate set is active.

**Implication for census methodology:** The census sweep should not just peak-detect each receiver's self-response. It should also check each receiver's response at every OTHER receiver's peak frequencies. This "cross-enrollment" captures the full Boolean-usable mode inventory.

### B. 39 Bits from 5 Plates Is the Current Hardware Limit

With 8 receivers across 5 plates, the maximum achievable Boolean compute is **39 bits at 100% fidelity**. This is 6 of 15 possible plate partitions. The limit comes from the total number of distinguishable frequency channels (39 unique frequencies where at least one side shows strong response and the other doesn't, or both do at discriminable levels).

### C. The 23→39 Jump: Two Sources

1. **Plate A (3→28 modes):** Adding plate A as an 8th receiver was huge. Cross-detection revealed 25 new modes invisible to A's original 3-peak census — A was responding strongly at D/H/G frequencies all along.
2. **Cross-detection on all receivers:** Even receivers already in the sweep (B, G, D, H) gained 10–27 new modes each. The per-receiver threshold detection in the push script (1.5M) is lower than the census peak-detection threshold, catching modes that were below the original SNR/prominence criteria but are still strong enough for Boolean discrimination.

### D. Prescan Is the Wrong Tool for Multi-Plate Boolean

The geometric-mean prescan threshold was designed for rod V5 where the mode floor is ~15K (close to noise). For plates, even the weakest detectable modes are ~1.3M — two orders of magnitude above noise. Prescan discards modes at the geometric mean (~3M) that are still perfectly usable. **For plates, the right approach is no-prescan with a lower per-receiver threshold (e.g., 50% of the weakest strong self-response, as the push script uses).**

### E. Bits Scale Sublinearly with Receivers

| Receivers             | Best Bits (100%) |
| --------------------- | ---------------- |
| 2                     | 13–14            |
| 4                     | 17–18            |
| 6                     | 22               |
| 7                     | 23               |
| 8 (with cross-detect) | 39               |

The jump from 7→8 receivers (23→39 bits) is anomalously large because it also included cross-detection. Without cross-detection, 8 receivers would likely give ~25 bits. The cross-detection itself accounts for roughly +14 bits by revealing previously hidden modes.

### F. Census Methodology Needs Updating

The current census captures each receiver's self-response peaks. But for Boolean compute, what matters is whether a receiver responds at ANY frequency with enough amplitude to discriminate. The census should be augmented with cross-enrollment: after the self-sweep, check each receiver's magnitude at all other receivers' peak frequencies. Any response >1.5M is a valid Boolean mode.

## 5. NARMA-10 Harmonic Ladder — Spatial Encoding of Temporal Memory

### Approach

Built `tools/narma_ladder.py` — a novel approach to the NARMA-10 benchmark that replaces temporal memory (which plates lack due to fast ringdown) with **spatial encoding**: all 10 input timesteps u(t-9)...u(t) are encoded simultaneously as amplitudes on 10 carrier frequencies, driven in a single multi-tone burst. The plate's nonlinear response generates intermodulation (IM) products at frequencies f_i ± f_j that encode pairwise products of inputs — exactly the kind of mixing NARMA-10 requires (especially the u(t-9)·u(t) term).

**Carrier selection:** 10 frequencies [19.0, 23.9, 29.2, 29.9, 34.55, 37.0, 45.0, 49.6, 56.2, 58.5] kHz chosen from harmonic ladder analysis to maximize IM2/IM3 products landing on known plate modes (40.7% hit rate; 62% for this best-10 set).

**Readout:** 117 frequencies — 10 carriers + 23 ambient modes + 44 IM2 + 40 IM3. 43 of 45 input-step pairs have IM coverage; the critical u(t-9)·u(t) pair is covered.

**Closed-loop extension:** 11th carrier at 11,425 Hz encodes y(t) feedback. Pass 1: teacher forcing (true y(t)). Pass 2: predicted ŷ(t) from Pass 1 ridge model.

### Hardware Results (300 steps, plate 4-NE, single receiver)

| Approach                   | Train NMSE | Test NMSE | Features | Notes                                         |
| -------------------------- | ---------- | --------- | -------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| mean_baseline              | 1.000      | 1.005     | 0        | —                                             |
| input_window_only          | 0.648      | **0.783** | 10       | Raw inputs, no plate                          |
| input_poly2                | 0.605      | 0.833     | 65       | Degree-2 polynomial of inputs                 |
| ladder_spectrum_only       | 0.498      | 1.034     | 117      | Plate spectrum only (overfits)                |
| ladder_full                | 0.466      | 0.945     | 127      | Spectrum + raw inputs                         |
| ladder_top20_poly_interact | 0.520      | 0.936     | 465      | Top-20 spectral + poly interactions           |
| **software_esn**           | **0.103**  | **0.171** | 64       | Software echo-state network                   |
| ladder_esn                 | 0.614      | 1.100     | 64       | ESN driven by plate spectrum                  |
| ladder_plus_esn_concat     | 0.288      | 0.885     | 191      | Concat plate + ESN features                   |
| ladder_feedback_openloop   | 0.000      | 0.000     | 147      | Teacher forcing (trivial — y(t) is a feature) |
| ladder_feedback_closedloop | 0.000      | 1.062     | 147      | Closed-loop diverges (error accumulation)     |

### Interpretation

1. **Software ESN dominates** at NMSE 0.171 — a legitimate NARMA-10 result with 64 recurrent features. This matches the simulation prediction exactly (sim: 0.171).

2. **Plate ladder provides nonlinear mixing** — train NMSE 0.466 (ladder_full) vs 0.648 (input_window_only) shows the plate spectral features encode input-dependent nonlinear functions. But 117 noisy spectral features overfit with only 201 training samples → test NMSE 0.945.

3. **No plate advantage over raw inputs on test set.** The best plate-only result (ladder_full test 0.945) is worse than simple raw inputs (0.783). The plate's IM products ARE there (evidenced by train improvement), but measurement noise overwhelms the signal for generalization.

4. **Closed-loop feedback fails** (1.062) — identical to simulation (1.059). The ridge model's prediction errors compound when fed back through the physical system. This is expected: closed-loop RC requires either high-quality reservoir dynamics or explicit error correction.

5. **Plate adds nothing to ESN** — ladder_plus_esn_concat (0.885) is far worse than ESN alone (0.171), and ladder_esn (1.100) is worse than baseline. The noisy plate features dilute the clean ESN state.

### Comparison to Previous NARMA-10 Attempt

Previous `tools/plate_narma10.py` used sequential single-tone drives and temporal ringdown:

- Plate raw: NMSE **1.025** (worse than baseline)
- Software ESN: NMSE **0.666**

The harmonic ladder approach improved plate-only from 1.025 → 0.945 (marginal), and software ESN from 0.666 → 0.171 (large, likely due to better ESN hyperparameters). **The spatial encoding idea works in principle (train NMSE shows real nonlinear mixing) but the SNR of plate IM products through a single PicoScope capture is insufficient for generalization.**

### What Would Help

- **More training data** (1000+ steps instead of 201) to regularize the 117 spectral features
- **Multi-receiver readout** (using all 5 NE receivers per step) — 5× more features with independent noise
- **Higher drive amplitude** or resonant carrier selection to boost IM product SNR
- **Averaging** more captures per step (currently N_AVG=8; could try 32+)

3. **Overfitting eliminated but not the problem.** `ladder_full` improved from 0.945 → 0.726 with 10× data. Train-test gap shrank from 0.48 to 0.05. But plate features still don't beat raw inputs alone. More data doesn't help when the fundamental issue is memoryless computation.

**Implication for the project:** The plate is a proven nonlinear computer (finding 1). The path to a NARMA-10 breakthrough requires making it a _temporal_ reservoir by exploiting Q-factor memory at ≥400 Hz step rates (section 7). At current 0.82 Hz, the plate is a static f(u(t)) transform — useful but memoryless.

### 10. Temporal Reservoir Investigation — Streaming & DDS Bottleneck

**Goal:** Build a temporal reservoir at ≥400 Hz to exploit Q-factor memory (τ ≈ 2ms for best modes).

#### 10a. narma_temporal.py — First attempt (58 Hz)

Built `tools/narma_temporal.py` — drives AWG at high-Q carrier (29.9 kHz, Q=218, τ=1.99ms), changes amplitude per step, captures 256 samples in block mode, extracts features via I/Q demod or FFT bands.

**Result:** 58.3 Hz step rate (17.15 ms/step). Zero temporal memory detected — shuffle test delta = −0.002. All temporal features indistinguishable from noise. Step period 8.6× longer than mode decay time → 0% retention.

#### 10b. Streaming API — Dead end

Investigated ps2000 streaming mode to bypass block capture overhead:

- `ps2000_run_streaming_ns`: works, captures data at 781 kHz continuously
- Callback signature: `CFUNCTYPE(None, POINTER(POINTER(c_int16)), c_int16, c_uint32, c_int16, c_int16, c_uint32)`
- **CRITICAL FINDING:** `ps2000_set_sig_gen_built_in` returns 0 (error) during streaming. Confirmed with controlled 500ms HIGH/LOW/HIGH test — RMS completely flat at ~228 across all phases. The ps2000 firmware locks out the DDS while the ADC is streaming.

**Verdict:** Streaming mode cannot be used for temporal reservoir because we cannot change the drive signal.

#### 10c. USB Latency Benchmarking

Measured individual ps2000 call latencies:

| Call                        | Mean        | Median     | p95    |
| --------------------------- | ----------- | ---------- | ------ |
| `set_sig_gen_built_in`      | 2.91ms      | 2.89ms     | 3.07ms |
| `capture_raw` (256 samples) | 3.37ms      | 3.05ms     | —      |
| **Theoretical minimum**     | **6.28ms**  | **5.94ms** | —      |
| **Actual (narma_temporal)** | **17.15ms** | —          | —      |

Gap of ~10.9ms was unexplained at this point.

#### 10d. Definitive Bottleneck Analysis — bench_step_rate.py

Created `tools/bench_step_rate.py` to decompose the 17ms step time. Results:

**Component timing (sig_gen → run_block → ready → get_values):**

| Component               | Time        |
| ----------------------- | ----------- |
| `set_sig_gen_built_in`  | 2.88ms      |
| `ps2000_run_block`      | 1.07ms      |
| **`ps2000_ready` wait** | **11.81ms** |
| `ps2000_get_values`     | 1.39ms      |
| numpy copy              | 0.008ms     |
| **TOTAL**               | **17.15ms** |

**The smoking gun:** `ps2000_ready` waits 11.8ms after a sig_gen call — the firmware stalls the ADC while the internal DDS updates.

**Elimination tests:**

| Test                             | Rate       | Conclusion                               |
| -------------------------------- | ---------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| sig_gen → capture (baseline)     | 57 Hz      | 17.2 ms/step                             |
| Capture only (no sig_gen)        | **431 Hz** | **2.3 ms/step**                          |
| Pipelined (capture then sig_gen) | 57 Hz      | DDS stall persists across iterations     |
| sig_gen → sleep(15ms) → capture  | —          | Capture STILL 14.3ms — not settling time |
| Frequency change only            | 58 Hz      | Same overhead as amplitude changes       |
| Pipelined component timing       | 56 Hz      | capture=14.7ms + sig_gen=3.0ms           |

**Root cause:** The ps2000 firmware internally serialises DDS updates and ADC block captures. Any `set_sig_gen_built_in` call (amplitude OR frequency change) triggers a ~12ms internal process that blocks the next ADC acquisition. This is a **hardware/firmware limitation** — no software optimisation can overcome it.

**Temporal memory at 58 Hz (17ms/step):**

| Mode             | τ (ms) | Retention/step |
| ---------------- | ------ | -------------- |
| 29.9 kHz (Q=218) | 1.99   | 0.02%          |
| 19.0 kHz (Q=156) | 2.07   | 0.03%          |
| 68.2 kHz (Q=296) | 1.49   | 0.01%          |

All modes: zero retention. **Temporal reservoir is not feasible at 58 Hz with current mode Q-factors.**

#### 10e. What Would Work

For meaningful temporal memory (>5% retention per step), we need either:

1. **Faster DDS:** External DDS chip (e.g., AD9833 via Arduino SPI) for drive signal, PicoScope for capture only at 431 Hz → retention 32% at τ=1.99ms
2. **Higher Q modes:** Modes at lower frequencies with Q ≈ 500+ would give τ > 17ms, making even 58 Hz viable. Low-frequency bending modes (< 2 kHz) are candidates.
3. **Different hardware:** PicoScope 2000a series has independent sig_gen, or use external waveform generator with fast command interface

**For now: the spatial (multi-plate) approach is the proven winner.** ladder_resid_esn = 0.171 NMSE, beating software ESN (0.197) by 13%.

#### 11g. Interpretation

1. **Inter-plate interference does not create useful new features.** The hypothesis was that 5 plates vibrating simultaneously would produce nonlinear mode coupling (inter-plate IM products, beating patterns) enriching the spectral response. Instead, the summed signal averages: each plate contributes its individual response at reduced amplitude, and the combined IM products are not distinguishable from single-plate IM products already captured at D-NE.

2. **No curse of dimensionality (unlike MUX).** All-open keeps the same 127 features — no overfitting penalty. But also no information gain. The 5 plates' contributions are linearly superposed on a single ADC channel, so any inter-plate nonlinearity must occur acoustically (not electrically). The plates are mechanically isolated on the PCB → no acoustic coupling path → purely linear superposition.

3. **The bottleneck is confirmed as temporal memory, not spatial diversity.** Both multi-plate approaches (MUX with 585 features, all-open with 127 features) produce the same `ladder_resid_esn` performance (~0.170). Adding more plates doesn't help because the NARMA-10 task requires remembering past states — something no amount of spatial diversity can provide at memoryless step rates.
