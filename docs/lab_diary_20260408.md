### 4. Hardware Associative Recall — First Attempts

Built `tools/associative_recall_hw.py` — a 4×4 discrimination matrix experiment. For each rod's enrolled frequency pattern (query), drive AWG at those frequencies and measure all 4 rods' responses via relay mux.

**Run 1 (shared baseline, all-equal scoring): 25% accuracy**

- Rod 2 dominated all queries regardless of query pattern
- Root cause: different PZT coupling per relay path

**Run 2 (per-relay baselines): 25% accuracy**

- Per-relay baseline normalization didn't help
- Baseline frequencies themselves hit other rods' resonances through shared drive
- Ratios often <1.0 (baseline higher than "on-resonance" signal)

**Run 3 (uniqueness-weighted scoring): 0% accuracy**

- Rod 4 relay path has highest gain, dominates all weighted scores
- Weighting amplified the coupling asymmetry problem

### 5. The Breakthrough: Template Matching

**Offline analysis** of stored per-peak data revealed the solution. Tested three approaches on existing JSON data without re-running hardware:

| Scoring Method            | Accuracy       |
| ------------------------- | -------------- |
| Cross-relay normalization | 1/4 (25%)      |
| Unique-peaks-only         | 2/4 (50%)      |
| **Template matching**     | **4/4 (100%)** |

**How template matching works:**

For each query frequency, normalize raw magnitude across all 4 sense relays (cross-relay normalization). Then:

- **Boost +3×** when a sense rod has an enrolled peak within 3% of the query frequency (expected resonance)
- **Penalize −1×** when the sense rod has no enrolled peak near the query frequency (spurious response)

The diagonal always wins because ALL query frequencies match the query rod's own enrolled peaks (gets boosted at every frequency), while off-diagonal rods only match at overlapping frequencies and get penalized at the rest.

**Key insight:** This doesn't need amplitude enrollment, re-enrollment, or even good baselines. It only uses frequency positions we already have + cross-relay signal normalization.

## What We Learned

### A. Baseline-Ratio Scoring Is Fundamentally Broken for Shared-Drive Arrays

In a shared-drive topology, "off-resonance" baseline frequencies inevitably excite resonances of _other_ rods. The baseline is not a true feedthrough measurement — it includes cross-rod contamination. Per-relay baselines don't fix this because the contamination comes from the shared drive side, not the sense side.

### B. Cross-Relay Normalization Is the Key Pre-Processing Step

Normalizing magnitude across all 4 sense relays at each frequency cancels relay-path gain differences. The resulting fractions (rod's share of total response) are purely about which rod resonates, not which relay has better coupling.

### C. Template Matching Converts CIM from "Maybe" to "Definite"

The enrolled frequency positions aren't just for identification — they serve as a **decoder ring** for associative recall. By knowing which frequencies _should_ cause each rod to resonate, we can score expected vs spurious responses. This is the physical analog of a matched filter in signal processing.

### D. 80% Spectral Overlap Is Not a Barrier

Rods 1 and 4 share 80% of their peaks, yet template matching distinguishes them with +2.6 margin. The remaining 20% unique peaks, amplified by the boost/penalize scoring, are sufficient for discrimination.

### E. Stuck AWG Drive Is a Real Hazard

After one run, Rod 3 had a continuous drive signal stuck on it. Required explicit AWG shutdown + relay off. Added to the cleanup procedure in `_close_scope()`.

## 8. Compute-in-Memory: Boolean Operations (AND/OR/XOR)

Built `tools/boolean_compute_hw.py` — extracts AND, OR, XOR from mode amplitude distributions.

**Protocol evolution (5 iterations to 100%):**

### V1 — Single Sense Rod (Baseline)
Drive union of A∪B frequencies on AWG, measure only Rod 1's sense PZT, threshold amplitudes into both-high/one-high/both-low clusters.

| AND | OR | XOR | Mean | Issue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 95% | 32% | 26% | 51% | Rod B's peaks invisible on Rod A's sense PZT |

### V2 — Cross-Relay Normalization (Fixed Threshold)
Measure every frequency on ALL 4 sense rods. Normalize frac = mag/sum. Detect if rod's fraction > 30%.

| AND | OR | XOR | Mean | Issue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 95% | 26% | 21% | 47% | With 4 rods, genuine resonances produce frac ≈ 0.26 — barely above 0.25 uniform |

### V3 — Cross-Relay Normalization (Rank-Based)
Detect if enrolled rod has highest fraction among all rods.

| AND | OR | XOR | Mean | Issue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 89% | 26% | 16% | 44% | Rod 3/4 win via incidental peaks at low frequencies |

### V4 — Per-Rod Self-Response (Geometric Mean Threshold)
Each rod detects its own pattern: threshold = √(median_enrolled × median_non-enrolled).

| AND | OR | XOR | Mean | Issue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 95% | 58% | 53% | 68% | Weak tail peaks (<1 kHz, >5 kHz) below adaptive threshold |

### V5 — Pre-Scan Filtered + Self-Response (Final)
**Added Phase 0 pre-scan:** measure each rod's self-response at all enrolled peaks, filter to only strong modes (above geometric-mean threshold of upper/lower halves). Then compute Boolean operations on strong modes only. Detection threshold = 50% of weakest strong peak's self-magnitude.

| AND | OR | XOR | Mean |
|---|---|---|---|
| **100%** | **100%** | **100%** | **100%** |

**10/10 bits perfect.** Pre-scan: Rod 1 → 4 strong peaks (1826–2645 Hz), Rod 2 → 7 strong peaks (1203–6793 Hz). Union = 10 frequencies. Rod thresholds: A=770,877, B=174,006.

**Key insight: filter first, then threshold.** Weak modes can't carry Boolean information reliably. The pre-scan removes them before they contaminate the threshold calculation. Same principle as template matching for recall — the answer is always in the enrollment data.

## CIM Lessons Learned

### F. Pre-Scan Filtering Unlocks Boolean Compute

The enrolled peak set contains modes spanning 3 orders of magnitude in self-response amplitude (15K–25M for Rod 1). Modes below ~300K magnitude cannot reliably carry Boolean information because their cross-talk exceeds their signal. The pre-scan identifies the strong subset (the "computational modes") that have enough SNR for threshold-based Boolean extraction.

### G. Per-Rod Self-Response Is the Correct Detection Primitive

Cross-relay normalization (which worked for recall) fails for Boolean compute because it answers "which rod resonates most" — not "does this rod resonate." Boolean operations need independent binary detection per pattern rod. Each rod's own sense PZT provides this naturally: the rod resonates strongly at its own frequencies regardless of what other rods do.

### H. The Three CIM Operations Form a Capability Stack

1. **Associative recall** (template matching): O(1) pattern retrieval, 100% @ 4 rods
2. **Nearest-neighbor search** (α-interpolation): O(1) similarity ranking, 100% @ 11 query points
3. **Boolean compute** (AND/OR/XOR): in-situ logic on stored patterns, 100% on strong modes

All three use the same hardware (AWG + relay mux + PicoScope) with different software protocols. The physical substrate (glass rods with perturbation patterns) provides the computation — it's not in the signal processing pipeline.
