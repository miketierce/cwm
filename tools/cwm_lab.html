⚛️ Quantum-Classical Bridge

CWM delivers three properties usually cited as motivations for quantum computing — superposition, interference-based computation, and non-destructive parallel readout — but achieves them classically via eigenmode orthogonality at room temperature. No cryogenics. No decoherence. No error correction. Each demo below runs on your real PicoScope hardware (or in simulation).

1
Classical Superposition — Many Channels, One Rod
Qubit Superposition CWM Eigenmodes ▶
A quantum computer uses qubit superposition to hold multiple states simultaneously. CWM does the same thing classically: a single glass rod vibrates in N independent eigenmodes at once. Each mode carries its own amplitude — the rod is literally in all states simultaneously. The mathematical basis is identical: orthogonal basis functions that don't interfere.
Quantum
|ψ⟩ = α₁|1⟩ + α₂|2⟩ + … + αₙ|N⟩
Requires millikelvin cooling
Coherence time: ~100 µs (superconducting)
Measurement collapses superposition
CWM (Your Hardware)
u(x,t) = Σ Aₙ sin(nπx/L) cos(2πfₙt)
Operates at 300 K (room temperature)
Ring-down Q > 10,000 (seconds of coherence)
FFT readout preserves all modes
🔬 Live Demo — Tap the rod, see all modes at once

A single impulse excites ALL eigenmodes simultaneously. The FFT reveals every mode's amplitude without destroying any of them — classical superposition in action.

2
Non-Destructive Readout — Measure Without Disturbing
QND Measurement Eigenmode Orthogonality ▶
In quantum systems, measurement generally disturbs the state — the "measurement problem" that drives error-correction overhead. CWM's eigenmode orthogonality provides a classical analogue of quantum non-demolition readout: driving at frequency fₙ couples exclusively to mode n because ∫₀ᴸ sin(nπx/L)·sin(mπx/L) dx = 0 for n ≠ m. This isn't approximate — it's a mathematical identity.
Quantum
Measurement collapses |ψ⟩ to one eigenstate
Requires error-correcting codes (surface code etc.)
~1,000 physical qubits per logical qubit
Read once, state destroyed
CWM (Your Hardware)
FFT reads ALL modes simultaneously
No error correction needed
1 rod = 1 rod (no overhead)
Read repeatedly, state unchanged
🔬 Live Demo — Repeated readout stability

Configure, then capture the spectrum multiple times in succession. Each readout should show the same eigenmode amplitudes — proving the measurement doesn't disturb the superposition. The correlation between consecutive reads quantifies QND fidelity.

O(1) Parallel Search — All Patterns at Once
Grover's Algorithm O(√N) Wave-Interference O(1) ▶
Grover's algorithm searches N items in O(√N) operations — a quadratic quantum speedup. CWM searches in O(1): drive all rods with a query, every rod computes its dot product via wave interference in a single acoustic propagation cycle (~3.8 µs). The computation time is independent of the number of patterns.
Quantum (Grover)
100k items → √100,000 ≈ 316 iterations
Each iteration ≈ 1 µs (gate time)
Total: ~316 µs + overhead
Requires coherent qubit register
CWM (Your 4-Rod Array)
All rods driven simultaneously
Single acoustic cycle: 3.8 µs
Independent of pattern count
Works at room temperature

4
Room-Temperature Coherence — No Decoherence Anxiety
Decoherence ~100 µs Ring-Down Q > 10,000 ▶
Quantum computers require dilution refrigerators at ~15 mK because quantum coherence decays in microseconds at room temperature. CWM operates at 300 K because hf ≪ kBT — the system lives deep in the classical Rayleigh-Jeans limit. The Q factor of glass rods determines information lifetime: Q > 10,000 for borosilicate, > 100,000 for fused silica. This translates to seconds of usable ring-down — 10⁷× longer than superconducting qubit coherence.
Quantum
Temperature: 15 mK (dilution fridge)
Coherence: ~100 µs (state-of-art)
Power: ~25 kW (refrigeration)
Cost: $10M+ per system
CWM (Your Hardware)
Temperature: 300 K (room temp)
Ring-down: seconds (Q > 10,000)
Power: ~50 mW (PicoScope + rod)
Cost: ~$100 (lab prototype)
🔬 Live Demo — Ring-down coherence measurement

Excite the rod and capture the ring-down envelope. The exponential decay rate gives the Q factor directly: Q = π·f·τ where τ is the e-folding decay time. Compare this to quantum coherence times.

5
Eigenmode Orthogonality — The Identity That Makes It Work
⟨n|m⟩ = δₙₘ ∫ sin·sin dx = 0 ▶
Quantum orthogonality (⟨n|m⟩ = δₙₘ) and CWM eigenmode orthogonality (∫₀ᴸ sin(nπx/L)·sin(mπx/L) dx = 0) are the same mathematical structure. This is why CWM can deliver quantum-like capabilities: the physics of standing waves in glass satisfies the same orthogonality relations as quantum energy eigenstates. This demo computes the cross-mode coupling matrix from your real hardware data.
🔬 Live Demo — Cross-mode coupling matrix

Capture the spectrum, then compute the mode-to-mode cross-correlation matrix. A perfect system shows an identity matrix (diagonal = 1, off-diagonal = 0). Real hardware shows how close your rod comes to perfect orthogonality.
